If you have lost money to a crypto scam, the pain is real and the instinct to get it back is completely understandable. That instinct is exactly what a crypto recovery scam preys on. A whole industry of fake "recovery agents" exists to find people at their lowest point and take whatever is left. This guide explains, calmly and honestly, why almost every crypto recovery service that contacts you is itself a scam and what genuinely helps after you have been targeted. Some of it is hard to hear, but you deserve the truth rather than false hope.

## The hard truth about crypto "recovery" services

When funds leave your wallet or are sent to a fraudulent platform, they usually move across the blockchain very quickly, often through mixers, bridges and exchanges in other jurisdictions. Once that has happened, reversing it is extremely difficult and frequently impossible. There is no undo button and no central authority that can claw a confirmed transaction back.

That reality is the foundation for one simple rule: **no legitimate service can guarantee recovery, and no legitimate service charges you an upfront fee to get your money back.** Anyone promising otherwise is either misinformed or lying.

This does not mean nothing can ever be done. The honest answer is "it depends, and often it is no" rather than "yes, pay us and we will fix it." The people who understand blockchain best are usually the most cautious about what is achievable.

## How recovery scams work (the second scam)

Recovery fraud is often called the "second scam" because it targets people who have already been defrauded once. The pattern is depressingly consistent:

- **They find you when you are vulnerable.** Scammers buy or scrape lists of victims, monitor complaint forums and social media, and cold-contact people who have posted about losing money.
- **They pose as authority.** Fake "blockchain forensics firms", bogus law enforcement, pretend regulators or imitation lawyers all lend a false sense of legitimacy.
- **They show you "proof".** You may be shown a dashboard that appears to display your recovered funds, fabricated case numbers, or screenshots of supposed seizures. None of it is real.
- **They ask for money to release the money.** First an "upfront fee", then a "tax", a "release charge", a "wallet activation cost" or a "bribe for the exchange". The demands keep coming as long as you keep paying.
- **They ask for access.** Some try to obtain your seed phrase, wallet passwords, or remote control of your device, which lets them empty whatever you have left.

The cruelty is the design: the more you have already lost, the more motivated you are to believe them. Recognising the pattern is your best protection. Our breakdown of [how to spot a crypto scam](/blog/how-to-spot-crypto-scam-2026) covers many of the same manipulation tactics in more detail.

## Warning signs of a fake recovery service

Treat any of the following as a serious red flag. The presence of even one is reason to walk away.

- **They contacted you first.** Genuine help is something you seek out; scammers come to you, often by DM, comment, email or phone.
- **They guarantee results** or speak in certainties about getting your funds back. No honest professional can promise this.
- **They want payment upfront**, especially in crypto, gift cards or via untraceable transfers.
- **They ask for your seed phrase or private keys.** No legitimate party ever needs these. Anyone who asks is trying to steal from you.
- **They create urgency.** "Act now or the window closes" is pressure designed to stop you thinking.
- **They cannot be verified.** No registered company details, no checkable regulatory authorisation, vague or stolen credentials, and a brand-new web presence.
- **They claim insider connections** to an exchange, a court or the police that let them bypass normal process.

## What actually helps after a scam

Concrete, boring, official steps are far more valuable than any miracle agent. Work through these as calmly as you can.

- **Report it to your national fraud body.** In the UK, report to Action Fraud (the national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime); in other countries, report to the equivalent police or fraud authority. Reporting builds the intelligence picture that occasionally leads to action and helps protect others.
- **Contact your bank or card provider immediately.** If you paid via bank transfer or card, speed matters. They may be able to attempt a recall or flag the receiving account, and they can secure your accounts against further loss.
- **Tell the exchange involved.** If funds passed through a regulated exchange, report the wallet addresses and transaction IDs. Exchanges can sometimes freeze assets that are still on their platform if alerted quickly. Our [crypto exchanges guide](/crypto-exchanges) explains how reputable platforms handle abuse reports.
- **Secure everything.** Move any remaining funds to a fresh, secure wallet with a brand-new seed phrase. Change passwords, enable strong two-factor authentication, and never reuse a seed phrase that may have been exposed. Our [crypto wallets guide](/crypto-wallets) walks through doing this safely.
- **Document everything.** Save transaction hashes, wallet addresses, screenshots, chat logs, names, websites and amounts with dates. This record is what any legitimate investigation will rely on.
- **Be wary of "help" that appears next.** The moment you report a loss publicly, recovery scammers may circle. Assume unsolicited offers are fraudulent.

## Can stolen crypto ever be recovered?

Sometimes, but rarely, and almost never through a service that cold-contacts you. Genuine recoveries tend to involve law enforcement operations or court orders against identified exchanges that still hold the funds. These processes are slow, are not guaranteed, and do not run on upfront fees paid to anonymous strangers.

It is important to hold two things at once: do not give up your right to report and pursue legitimate channels, but also protect yourself from being hurt again by accepting that, for most individual victims, recovery is unlikely. Both can be true.

Being scammed is not a reflection of your intelligence. These operations are professional, well-resourced and deliberately manipulative. The most powerful thing you can do now is refuse the second scam, take the official steps above, and share what happened so the next person spots it sooner.
