If you've lost money to a crypto scam, you want it back. Of course you do. That instinct is human and reasonable, and it's exactly what a crypto recovery scam is built to exploit. There's a whole grubby industry of fake "recovery agents" who hunt for people at their lowest moment and take whatever is left. This guide explains, calmly and straight, why almost every recovery service that contacts you is itself a scam, and what actually helps once you've been hit. Some of it is hard to read. You deserve the truth more than comfort that isn't real.

## The hard truth about crypto "recovery" services

When funds leave your wallet or land on a fraudulent platform, they tend to move across the blockchain fast, hopping through mixers, bridges and exchanges in jurisdictions that don't pick up the phone. Once that's happened, reversing it is extremely difficult and often impossible. No undo button, no central authority to claw a confirmed transaction back.

That gives us one simple rule: no legitimate service can guarantee recovery, and none charges you an upfront fee to get your money back. Anyone telling you otherwise is either out of their depth or lying to your face.

This doesn't mean nothing can ever be done. The honest answer is "it depends, and often it's no," not "yes, pay us and we'll sort it." The people who genuinely understand blockchain are the most careful about what's possible. The ones promising the world are eyeing your wallet.

## How recovery scams work (the second scam)

Recovery fraud gets called the "second scam" because it targets people already defrauded once. The playbook is grimly predictable:

- **They find you when you're vulnerable.** They buy or scrape lists of victims, lurk on complaint forums and social media, and cold-contact anyone who's posted about a loss. Your pain is their lead generation.
- **They pose as authority.** Fake "blockchain forensics firms", bogus law enforcement, pretend regulators, imitation lawyers. The whole costume box, borrowed to look respectable.
- **They show you "proof".** A dashboard that seems to display your recovered funds, fabricated case numbers, screenshots of seizures that never happened. It's stagecraft, every pixel.
- **They ask for money to release the money.** First an "upfront fee", then a "tax", a "release charge", a "wallet activation cost", maybe a "bribe for the exchange". The demands never stop while you keep paying.
- **They ask for access.** Some go for your seed phrase, wallet passwords, or remote control of your device, which lets them empty whatever you've managed to hang onto.

The cruelty is the design: the more you've already lost, the more you want to believe them, and they know it. Spotting the pattern is your best armour. Our breakdown of [how to spot a crypto scam](/blog/how-to-spot-crypto-scam-2026) digs into the same tricks in more detail.

## Warning signs of a fake recovery service

Treat any one of these as a serious red flag. You only need one to walk away.

- **They contacted you first.** Real help is something you seek out. Scammers come to you, by DM, comment, email or phone.
- **They guarantee results,** or talk in certainties about getting your funds back. No honest professional can promise that.
- **They want payment upfront,** especially in crypto, gift cards or untraceable transfers.
- **They ask for your seed phrase or private keys.** Nobody legitimate ever needs these. Anyone who asks is trying to rob you.
- **They manufacture urgency.** "Act now or the window closes" is pressure, engineered to stop you thinking clearly.
- **They can't be verified.** No registered company details, no checkable authorisation, vague or stolen credentials, a website that went live last Tuesday.
- **They claim insider connections** to an exchange, a court or the police that magically let them skip the rules everyone else follows.

## What actually helps after a scam

The boring, official steps are worth far more than any miracle agent. Work through them as calmly as you can.

- **Report it to your national fraud body.** In the UK, that's Action Fraud, the national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime. Elsewhere, your equivalent police or fraud authority. Reporting builds the intelligence picture that occasionally leads to action, and it protects the next person.
- **Contact your bank or card provider immediately.** If you paid by bank transfer or card, speed matters. They may be able to attempt a recall or flag the receiving account, and lock your accounts against further loss.
- **Tell the exchange involved.** If funds passed through a regulated exchange, hand over the wallet addresses and transaction IDs. Exchanges can sometimes freeze assets still on their platform, if alerted fast. Our [crypto exchanges guide](/crypto-exchanges) covers how reputable platforms handle abuse reports.
- **Secure everything.** Move any remaining funds to a fresh wallet with a brand-new seed phrase. Change passwords, switch on strong two-factor authentication, and never reuse a seed phrase that may have been exposed. Our [crypto wallets guide](/crypto-wallets) walks you through this safely.
- **Document everything.** Save transaction hashes, wallet addresses, screenshots, chat logs, names, websites and amounts, with dates. It's what any genuine investigation will lean on.
- **Be wary of the "help" that shows up next.** The moment you report a loss publicly, recovery scammers start circling. Assume every unsolicited offer is fraudulent until proven otherwise.

## Can stolen crypto ever be recovered?

Sometimes. Rarely. And almost never through a service that cold-contacts you. Real recoveries tend to involve law enforcement operations or court orders against identified exchanges still holding the funds. Those processes are slow, carry no guarantees, and don't run on upfront fees paid to strangers in your DMs.

So hold two things at once. Don't surrender your right to report and pursue legitimate channels. But protect yourself by accepting that, for most individual victims, recovery is unlikely. Both are true, and pretending otherwise only helps the next scammer.

One last thing, and it matters. Being scammed says nothing about how clever you are. These operations are professional, well-funded and run by people who manipulate for a living. The strongest move now is to refuse the second scam, take the official steps above, and tell someone what happened so the next person clocks it sooner.
