Crypto blockchains are the rails that everything else in this space runs on. When you buy a token, move funds between platforms, or interact with an app, you are trusting a particular chain to record what happened and to keep that record honest. Yet the chain itself is often the part people understand least. This guide explains, in plain terms, how crypto blockchains differ, what makes one sturdier than another, and why picking a well-established chain reduces some risks but never removes the need to scrutinise the specific project or token you are dealing with.

## What a blockchain is and why the chain matters

A blockchain is a shared ledger maintained by many independent computers rather than a single company. Transactions are grouped into blocks, cryptographically linked, and confirmed by a network of participants according to agreed rules. Once enough of the network has confirmed a block, rewriting it becomes extremely difficult, which is what gives a blockchain its tamper-resistance.

The chain matters because it sets the conditions you operate under. It determines how quickly transactions settle, how much they cost in fees, how exposed you are to outages, and how hard it would be for any single party to censor or reverse activity. A chain with thousands of independent validators spread around the world behaves very differently from one run by a handful of insiders, even if both call themselves "decentralised".

Crucially, the chain is the foundation, not the building. A reputable blockchain can still host fraudulent tokens, broken applications and outright scams. Settlement on a trusted chain tells you the transaction happened; it tells you nothing about whether the project you sent funds to is legitimate.

## Types of network

Crypto blockchains are commonly grouped by the role they play. Understanding the categories helps you read what a platform is actually offering and where the trade-offs sit.

### Layer 1 blockchains

A Layer 1 is a base chain that settles its own transactions and provides its own security. Bitcoin and Ethereum are the most widely cited examples. Layer 1 chains carry the full weight of validating activity, which is why they tend to be the most battle-tested but can become slow or expensive when demand is high.

Layer 1 designs vary considerably. Some prioritise decentralisation and security at the cost of speed; others optimise for high throughput and low fees, often by accepting a smaller or more concentrated validator set. Neither approach is automatically "better", but the trade-off is real, and you should know which one you are relying on. If you plan to hold assets on a chain long term, the security and maturity of its Layer 1 should weigh heavily, and you will want a [crypto wallet](/crypto-wallets) that genuinely supports it.

### Layer 2 scaling networks

Layer 2 networks are built on top of a Layer 1 to make transactions faster and cheaper while still, in principle, inheriting the base chain's security. They typically process activity off the main chain and periodically post compressed proofs or summaries back to the Layer 1 that anchors them.

The appeal is lower fees and quicker confirmations, which is why a great deal of [DeFi platform](/defi-platforms) activity has migrated to Layer 2s. The caveats are worth keeping in mind: not every Layer 2 inherits security to the same degree, some rely on a single operator to sequence transactions, and the technology is younger than the Layer 1s beneath it. Treat a Layer 2's maturity and decentralisation with the same scrutiny you would apply to any chain.

### Bridges (and bridge risk)

Bridges connect separate chains so assets can move between them, for example from a Layer 1 to a Layer 2. They are useful, but they have repeatedly proved to be one of the most exploited components in crypto, because a bridge often holds large pools of locked funds that become a tempting target.

When you bridge, you are usually trusting the bridge's smart contracts and operators, not just the chains at either end. Favour bridges that are well audited, long-running and widely used, move modest amounts first, and remember that "wrapped" tokens on the destination chain are only as sound as the bridge backing them.

## How to judge a chain (security, decentralisation, track record)

No chain is flawless, so judging crypto blockchains is about resilience rather than perfection. A few questions cut through most marketing:

- **Decentralisation.** How many independent validators or miners secure the network, and how widely are they distributed? Greater, more genuine decentralisation makes censorship and tampering harder.
- **Security model.** What consensus mechanism does it use, and has that model been stress-tested at scale over time?
- **Track record.** How long has the chain run, and how has it coped with outages, congestion or attempted attacks? Longevity under real pressure is hard to fake.
- **Transparency and development.** Is the code open, is development active, and is governance clear rather than controlled by an anonymous few?
- **Ecosystem health.** Reliable infrastructure, reputable applications and honest documentation are all positive signs.

As a general rule, more decentralised and longer-running chains tend to be safer foundations than new, fast-growing networks with concentrated control. That is a tendency, not a guarantee, and even the most established chain cannot vouch for what is built on top of it.

## Risks that affect your safety (congestion, scam tokens, bridges)

Several chain-level risks can affect you directly, even when you have done nothing wrong:

- **Congestion and fees.** When a network is busy, fees can spike and transactions can stall. During volatile periods this can leave you unable to act when it matters most.
- **Scam tokens.** Anyone can typically create a token on a major chain. The chain's legitimacy lends no legitimacy to the token. Verify contract addresses from official sources and be wary of tokens you cannot independently confirm.
- **Bridge exploits.** As above, cross-chain bridges have been a recurring point of failure. Limit how much you keep in transit.
- **Smart-contract risk.** Apps on a chain can contain bugs or malicious code regardless of how sound the chain is. This applies to lending, swapping and [crypto staking](/crypto-staking) protocols alike.
- **Custody assumptions.** Where your assets actually live shapes your exposure. Our explainer on [self-custody versus custodial wallets](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026) walks through that choice, and many people move funds off [crypto exchanges](/crypto-exchanges) into self-custody for long-term holdings.

We assess crypto blockchains on evidence rather than promotion, and we flag uncertainty plainly where information is incomplete. Our review focuses on:
