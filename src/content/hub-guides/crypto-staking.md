Most "ways to earn" in crypto are smoke and mirrors. Crypto staking is one of the rare exceptions, because the money traces back to something real: the work of keeping a blockchain honest. The catch? The word "staking" has been nicked by platforms paying you from somewhere else. That gap, between the real thing and the costume version, is where most people get fleeced.

This guide explains how crypto staking actually works, the honest ways to do it, and the risks the marketing leaves out. No price predictions. No anointed winners. One idea for your back pocket: real staking pays modest, single-digit rewards funded by the network itself. Fixed, double-digit "staking" returns are usually a riskier product wearing staking's clothes.

## What staking actually is (where the yield comes from)

Loads of blockchains secure themselves through proof of stake. Instead of miners torching electricity, participants lock up the network's own token to back validators, the machines that propose and confirm blocks. Do the job honestly, earn rewards. Misbehave, get penalised. Staking is simply committing your tokens to that process, directly or through a service.

The rewards come from two honest sources:

- **Network issuance.** The protocol mints a controlled trickle of new tokens and hands them to the people helping secure it.
- **Transaction fees.** A slice of what users pay can flow to validators and, in turn, to you.

That's the whole engine. Issuance is capped by the protocol's rules and fees depend on real usage, so the yield has a natural ceiling: usually a low single-digit percentage that wobbles over time. When a return sits well above that range, ask where the extra is coming from. For how networks set these rules, our overview of [blockchains](/blockchains) is a sensible place to start.

## Types of staking

"Staking" covers arrangements with wildly different risk profiles. Knowing which one you're using matters far more than the headline rate.

### Solo staking

You run your own validator and stake directly. Maximum control, minimum middlemen, and a matching list of responsibilities: reliable hosting, near-constant uptime, careful key management, often a chunky minimum stake. Mess it up and the bill is yours alone. Great for confident techies. Not the gentle on-ramp newcomers want.

### Liquid staking

A protocol stakes for you and hands back a tradable token representing your position. You can put it to work elsewhere while still earning rewards. Genuinely handy. It also stacks on exposure: you're leaning on the protocol's smart contracts, and that token can trade below the asset it stands for. Reuse it across other platforms and you're piling risk on risk like a Jenga tower. Plenty of liquid staking services live inside the wider [DeFi platforms](/defi-platforms) ecosystem, with its own homework.

### Exchange staking

A centralised exchange stakes on your behalf and drops the rewards into your account. The path of least resistance. But you've handed over custody and you're betting on the exchange staying solvent and honest. Rates should still look like network rewards. If one dangles a fixed return miles above them, treat it as a separate product, not staking. Our guide to [crypto exchanges](/crypto-exchanges) covers how to size up a venue's transparency and track record.

### Pooled staking

Smaller holders club their funds together to clear a validator's minimum and split the rewards proportionally. It lowers the barrier, but bolts on an operator you must trust to run validators competently and handle penalties fairly. Look for plain disclosure of fees, validator performance, and how slashing losses get carved up.

## The risks (lock-ups, slashing, de-pegs, smart-contract risk)

Staking is not free money, and every route comes with hazards. The big ones:

- **Lock-ups and unbonding periods.** Staked tokens are often frozen for a fixed term, or take days to weeks to withdraw. If the price tanks during that window, you're stuck watching.
- **Slashing.** Validators that go offline or play dirty can have part of their stake destroyed, and that loss can reach you.
- **De-pegs.** A liquid staking token can drift below the asset it represents, especially when markets get jumpy.
- **Smart-contract risk.** Liquid and pooled staking ride on code, and code can carry bugs or be exploited. Audits shrink that risk; they don't erase it.
- **Custodial and counterparty risk.** With exchange and some pooled options, the operator going bust or rogue can cost you the lot.

Because so much of this risk lives in custody and keys, your security habits matter. Our guide to [crypto wallets](/crypto-wallets) covers seed-phrase hygiene and the approvals liquid staking demands.

## Real yields vs "too good to be true"

This is where the real damage gets done. Genuine staking yields are modest and move with the network, because issuance and fees are finite by design. A platform waving a high, fixed return and calling it "staking" is almost always doing something else, and that something else is usually the trapdoor.

Tells that a "staking" return isn't really staking:

- A fixed rate deep into double digits, sold as dependable whatever the market does.
- Returns paid mostly in a token the platform prints itself, with barely any outside demand.
- Hand-wavy talk of "arbitrage", "AI strategies" or mystery trading desks behind the yield.
- Pressure to lock funds fast, or bonuses for dragging your mates in.
- No clear, one-sentence answer to where the money comes from.

Any one of these earns a raised eyebrow. Several together usually means walk away.

That's the mechanics of ponzi yield, where today's returns are paid out of new deposits rather than real activity. We lay out the difference with worked examples in our analysis of [real yield versus ponzi yield](/blog/defi-lending-real-yield-vs-ponzi-yield-2026). If the explanation can't survive one question about its source, that's your answer.

## How we rate staking platforms

We don't rank by advertised rate. A big shiny number nobody can explain counts against a platform, not for it. We weigh evidence across a few areas:

- **Source of yield:** whether returns plausibly trace to issuance and fees, rather than token emissions or fresh deposits.
- **Custody and control:** who holds the keys, and what happens to your stake if the operator goes under.
- **Lock-up and exit terms:** how clearly unbonding periods, withdrawal delays and penalties are spelled out.
- **Security:** for liquid and pooled options, the quality and recency of independent audits.
- **Transparency and track record:** disclosure of validators, fees and slashing policy, plus conduct in ugly markets.

Where the evidence is thin or contradictory, we say so instead of guessing. Staking can be a sound way to earn from a network you already hold, but it rewards patience and scepticism. Expect modest rewards, check where they come from, and never stake more than you can afford to lose.
