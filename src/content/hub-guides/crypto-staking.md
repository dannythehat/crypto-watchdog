Crypto staking is one of the few ways to earn a return in this market that traces back to something real: the work of securing a blockchain. The trouble is that the word "staking" has been borrowed and abused by platforms that pay returns from somewhere else entirely, and the gap between the two is where most people lose money.

This guide explains how crypto staking actually works, the genuine ways to do it, and the risks marketing tends to skip. It makes no price predictions and names no winners. The single idea to carry through is simple: real staking pays modest, single-digit rewards funded by the network itself, while fixed, double-digit "staking" returns are usually a different and far riskier product wearing staking's clothes.

## What staking actually is (where the yield comes from)

Many blockchains secure themselves through proof of stake. Instead of miners burning electricity, participants lock up the network's own token to back validators that propose and confirm blocks. Honest work earns rewards; misbehaviour can be penalised. Staking is the act of committing your tokens to this process, directly or through a service.

The rewards come from two honest sources:

- **Network issuance.** The protocol mints a controlled amount of new tokens and distributes them to those who help secure it.
- **Transaction fees.** A share of the fees users pay can flow to validators and, in turn, to stakers.

That is the whole engine. Because issuance is capped by the protocol's rules and fees depend on real usage, the yield is naturally limited. It is typically a low single-digit percentage and it varies over time. A return well above that range is a signal to ask where the extra is really coming from. To understand how different networks set these rules, our overview of [blockchains](/blockchains) is a useful starting point.

## Types of staking

"Staking" covers several arrangements with very different risk profiles. Knowing which one you are using matters more than the headline rate.

### Solo staking

You run your own validator and stake directly. This gives you the most control and the fewest intermediaries, but it carries real responsibilities: reliable hosting, near-constant uptime, careful key management, and often a substantial minimum stake. Mistakes are yours to bear. It suits technically confident holders, not newcomers.

### Liquid staking

A protocol stakes on your behalf and issues a tradable token representing your position, which you can use elsewhere while still earning rewards. The convenience is real, but so is the added exposure: you now depend on the protocol's smart contracts, and the representative token can trade below the underlying asset. Reusing that token across other platforms stacks risk on risk. Many liquid staking services sit within the wider [DeFi platforms](/defi-platforms) ecosystem, which brings its own due-diligence demands.

### Exchange staking

A centralised exchange stakes for you and credits the rewards to your account. It is the simplest route, but you hand custody to the exchange and rely on its solvency and honesty. Rates here should still resemble network rewards; when an exchange offers a fixed return far above them, treat it as a separate product, not staking. Our guide to [crypto exchanges](/crypto-exchanges) covers how to judge a venue's transparency and track record.

### Pooled staking

Smaller holders combine funds to meet a validator's minimum and share the rewards proportionally. This lowers the barrier to entry, but introduces an operator you must trust to run validators competently and handle penalties fairly. Look for clear disclosure of fees, validator performance and how slashing losses would be allocated.

## The risks (lock-ups, slashing, de-pegs, smart-contract risk)

Staking is not free money, and every route carries hazards. The main ones are:

- **Lock-ups and unbonding periods.** Staked tokens are often illiquid for a fixed term, or take days to weeks to withdraw. If the price falls during that window, you cannot exit quickly.
- **Slashing.** Validators that go offline or behave dishonestly can have part of their stake destroyed, and that loss can reach you.
- **De-pegs.** A liquid staking token can drift below the asset it represents, especially under stress.
- **Smart-contract risk.** Liquid and pooled staking depend on code that can contain bugs or be exploited. Audits reduce this risk; they do not remove it.
- **Custodial and counterparty risk.** With exchange and some pooled options, the operator's failure or dishonesty can cost you everything.

Because much of this risk lives in custody and keys, your own security posture matters. Our guide to [crypto wallets](/crypto-wallets) covers seed-phrase hygiene and the approvals that liquid staking often requires.

## Real yields vs "too good to be true"

This is where most harm happens. Genuine staking yields are modest and move with the network, because they are funded by issuance and fees that are finite by design. A platform promising a high, fixed return labelled as "staking" is almost always doing something else, and that is usually where the danger lives.

Signs that an advertised "staking" return is not really staking:

- A fixed rate well into double digits, presented as dependable regardless of market conditions.
- Returns paid mainly in a token the platform prints itself, with little outside demand.
- Vague talk of "arbitrage", "AI strategies" or trading desks behind the yield.
- Pressure to lock funds quickly, or bonuses for recruiting others.
- No clear, one-sentence explanation of where the money comes from.

Each of these on its own warrants caution; several together should usually be disqualifying.

These are the mechanics of ponzi yield, where today's returns are paid from new deposits rather than real activity. We set out the distinction with worked examples in our analysis of [real yield versus ponzi yield](/blog/defi-lending-real-yield-vs-ponzi-yield-2026). If the explanation does not survive a question about its source, that is your answer.

## How we rate staking platforms

We do not rank by advertised rate, and a high number counts against a platform rather than for it when it cannot be explained. We weigh evidence across a few areas:

- **Source of yield:** whether returns plausibly trace to network issuance and fees rather than emissions or new deposits.
- **Custody and control:** who holds the keys, and what happens to your stake if the operator fails.
- **Lock-up and exit terms:** how clearly unbonding periods, withdrawal delays and penalties are disclosed.
- **Security:** for liquid and pooled options, the quality and recency of independent audits.
- **Transparency and track record:** disclosure of validators, fees and slashing policy, and conduct through volatile markets.

Where the evidence is thin or mixed, we say so plainly rather than guessing. Staking can be a sound way to earn from a network you already hold, but it rewards patience and scepticism. Expect modest rewards, verify where they come from, and never stake more than you can afford to lose.
