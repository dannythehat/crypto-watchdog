Pick the wrong exchange and the slick app won't save you. The platform you choose decides how easily you get your money out, who's holding your coins, and how badly you'll hurt if it goes sideways. Fees and pretty charts are a sideshow. This guide is about the rest.

## What to look for in the best crypto exchanges

Search for the best crypto exchanges and most people start counting fees and coins. Fair enough, but those are the wallpaper, not the foundations. The questions that keep you whole are duller: who holds your keys, and whether you can get your money out.

A few things worth tattooing somewhere visible:

- **Not your keys, not your coins.** If a platform holds your private keys, you're betting on it staying solvent and honest. Forever. Move to self-custody in a dedicated [crypto wallet](/crypto-wallets) and that dependency vanishes, but now you're the security team, with nobody to ring if it goes wrong.
- **Withdrawals are the only test that doesn't lie.** A platform can look glossy while quietly turning the cash-out tap tighter every week. Money leaving smoothly, every time, is the clearest sign of health.
- **Regulation is a floor, not a hug.** A licence trims some risks. It does not make a platform safe, and anyone telling you it does is selling something.

So the best crypto exchanges, to us, are the ones with believable custody, honest disclosures, and a boring habit of letting customers walk off with their own money on demand.

## What makes a crypto exchange safe

No exchange is without risk, so "safe" here means well-run, honest and hard to topple. What separates the sturdy from the wobbly:

- **Custody and reserves.** Does it explain how your assets are held and segregated, and publish proof-of-reserves or independent attestations? Or just say "trust us" in a nice font?
- **Regulatory standing.** Is it actually registered with the right authority where you live, and is that registration current, not just a logo nicked off a site?
- **Withdrawal reliability.** A real history of paying people out, especially when markets are on fire, beats any marketing line.
- **Security practices.** Two-factor authentication, withdrawal allowlists, sensible key storage and a clean incident record say grown-ups run the place.
- **Operational transparency.** Clear fees, named humans in charge, a real legal entity, support that replies. Dull as ditchwater, and telling.

A working sense of how [blockchains](/blockchains) settle transactions helps too. It's the difference between nodding along to claims about speed and fees and catching the nonsense.

## Types of crypto exchange

Different models, different headaches, each trading convenience against custody and counterparty risk.

### Centralised exchanges (CEX)

The familiar one. A company runs the order book, holds the funds, matches buyers to sellers. Easiest way in, with fiat on-ramps, support and deep liquidity. The catch is custodial risk: your coins live with them, so their solvency and honesty become your problem. This is "not your keys, not your coins" at its purest.

### Decentralised exchanges (DEX)

Here you trade straight from your own wallet through smart contracts, no middleman holding the bag. You keep your keys throughout, killing off one big way to lose everything. In return, you take on smart-contract risk, the chore of running your own wallet, and a learning curve that bites beginners. If you fancy this route, our rundown of [DeFi platforms](/defi-platforms) shows how these protocols work and where the trapdoors are.

### P2P exchanges

Peer-to-peer marketplaces put individual buyers and sellers together, with the platform often just holding funds in escrow. Handy for flexible payment methods and regions where banking is patchy. The trade-off: all your risk now sits on the stranger across the table. Reputation scores, escrow and confirming the payment landed aren't optional.

### Brokers

Brokers sell you crypto directly at a quoted price instead of matching you against other traders. Quick, simple, kind to beginners, but that convenience is baked into a wider spread. Check one thing first: can you withdraw to an external wallet, or only sell back to them? If it's the latter, you own an IOU, not much else.

## Red flags and withdrawal warning signs

The same patterns turn up across every platform that's gone bad. Treat any of these as a reason to stop and verify before another penny goes in:

- Withdrawals that drag, get capped, or suddenly demand verification nobody mentioned.
- Pressure to deposit more before you can withdraw, or "fees" and "taxes" to "release" your funds. That's not a process. That's a scam.
- Promises of fixed or outsized returns, locked-in profits, or anything sold to you as a sure thing. Run.
- Faceless operators, no verifiable legal entity, or a registration the regulator can't confirm.
- Reviews complaining, specifically, that people can't get their money out. Believe them.

If a withdrawal stalls, screenshot everything and stop topping up. Our [scam warnings](/warnings) section tracks the tactics these outfits use, and the moves rhyme even when the branding gets a fresh coat of paint.

## How to choose

Start by matching the model to your goal. Want self-custody? Lean toward a DEX with a wallet you control. Want simplicity and easy fiat access? A regulated CEX or broker might suit, as long as you've made peace with the trade-off. Then, before trusting anyone with real money, run a tiny dry run:

1. Deposit a modest amount.
2. Make a small trade.
3. **Withdraw to an external wallet** and watch it clear.

No platform is without risk, and the picture shifts. Re-check custody, regulatory standing and withdrawal performance now and then, keep only what you need on any exchange, and move long-term holdings to self-custody where you can.
