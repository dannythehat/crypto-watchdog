The exchange you pick matters more than most people realise. It decides how easily you get your money out, who's holding your coins, and how much you stand to lose if it goes wrong. Fees and charts are the easy part to compare. This guide is about everything else.

## What to look for in the best crypto exchanges

Search for the best crypto exchanges and most people start by counting fees and coins. Fair enough, but those matter less than they seem. The questions that actually keep you whole are duller: who holds your keys, and whether you can get your money out.

A few things worth keeping front of mind:

- **Not your keys, not your coins.** If a platform holds your private keys, you're relying on it to stay solvent and honest indefinitely. Move to self-custody in a dedicated [crypto wallet](/crypto-wallets) and that dependency vanishes, but then the security is entirely down to you, with no one to call if it goes wrong.
- **Withdrawals are the test that matters most.** A platform can look polished while quietly making it harder to cash out. Money leaving smoothly, every time, is the clearest sign of health.
- **Regulation is a floor, not a guarantee.** A licence reduces some risks. It does not make a platform safe, and anyone telling you otherwise is selling something.

So the best crypto exchanges, to us, are the ones with believable custody, honest disclosures, and a reliable record of letting customers withdraw their own money on demand.

## What makes a crypto exchange safe

No exchange is without risk, so "safe" here means well-run, honest and hard to topple. What separates the sturdy from the shaky:

- **Custody and reserves.** Does it explain how your assets are held and segregated, and publish proof-of-reserves or independent attestations? Or does it just ask you to trust it?
- **Regulatory standing.** Is it actually registered with the right authority where you live, and is that registration current, rather than just a logo copied onto the site?
- **Withdrawal reliability.** A real history of paying people out, especially when markets are volatile, beats any marketing line.
- **Security practices.** Two-factor authentication, withdrawal allowlists, sensible key storage and a clean incident record show the place is run properly.
- **Operational transparency.** Clear fees, named people in charge, a real legal entity, support that replies. Unglamorous, and telling.

A working sense of how [blockchains](/blockchains) settle transactions helps too. It's the difference between nodding along to claims about speed and fees and being able to spot when they don't add up.

## Types of crypto exchange

Different models come with different trade-offs, each balancing convenience against custody and counterparty risk.

### Centralised exchanges (CEX)

The familiar one. A company runs the order book, holds the funds, and matches buyers to sellers. It's the easiest way in, with fiat on-ramps, support and deep liquidity. The catch is custodial risk: your coins sit with them, so their solvency and honesty become your problem. This is "not your keys, not your coins" in its clearest form.

### Decentralised exchanges (DEX)

Here you trade straight from your own wallet through smart contracts, with no middleman holding your funds. You keep your keys throughout, which removes one major way to lose everything. In return, you take on smart-contract risk, the work of running your own wallet, and a learning curve that's tough on beginners. If this route appeals, our rundown of [DeFi platforms](/defi-platforms) shows how these protocols work and where the risks lie.

### P2P exchanges

Peer-to-peer marketplaces put individual buyers and sellers together, with the platform often just holding funds in escrow. Handy for flexible payment methods and regions where banking is patchy. The trade-off: most of your risk now rests on the person you're dealing with. Reputation scores, escrow and confirming the payment landed aren't optional.

### Brokers

Brokers sell you crypto directly at a quoted price instead of matching you against other traders. Quick, simple and beginner-friendly, but that convenience is built into a wider spread. Check one thing first: can you withdraw to an external wallet, or only sell back to them? If it's the latter, you don't really hold the crypto, only a claim against the broker.

## Red flags and withdrawal warning signs

The same patterns turn up across every platform that's gone bad. Treat any of these as a reason to stop and verify before another penny goes in:

- Withdrawals that drag, get capped, or suddenly demand verification nobody mentioned.
- Pressure to deposit more before you can withdraw, or "fees" and "taxes" to "release" your funds. That's not a process. That's a scam.
- Promises of fixed or outsized returns, locked-in profits, or anything sold to you as a sure thing. Run.
- Faceless operators, no verifiable legal entity, or a registration the regulator can't confirm.
- Reviews complaining, specifically, that people can't get their money out. Believe them.

If a withdrawal stalls, screenshot everything and stop topping up. Our [scam warnings](/warnings) section tracks the tactics these outfits use, and the moves rhyme even when the branding gets a fresh coat of paint.

## How to choose

Start by matching the model to your goal. Want self-custody? Lean toward a DEX with a wallet you control. Want simplicity and easy fiat access? A regulated CEX or broker might suit, as long as you've made peace with the trade-off. Then, before trusting anyone with real money, run a tiny dry run:

1. Deposit a modest amount.
2. Make a small trade.
3. **Withdraw to an external wallet** and watch it clear.

No platform is without risk, and the picture shifts. Re-check custody, regulatory standing and withdrawal performance now and then, keep only what you need on any exchange, and move long-term holdings to self-custody where you can.
