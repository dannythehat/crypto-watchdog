Pick the wrong exchange and the slick app won't save you. The platform you choose decides how easily you get your money back out, who's holding your coins, and how badly you'll hurt if it all goes sideways. Fees and pretty charts are a sideshow. This guide is about the bit that matters: not losing the lot.

## What to look for in the best crypto exchanges

Search for the best crypto exchanges and most people start counting fees and coins. Fair enough, those count. But they're the wallpaper, not the foundations. The questions that keep you whole are duller and far more important: who holds your keys, what they tell you, and whether you can take your money out when you ask.

A few things worth tattooing somewhere visible:

- **Not your keys, not your coins.** If a platform holds your private keys, you're betting on that company staying solvent and honest. Both. Forever. Move to self-custody in a dedicated [crypto wallet](/crypto-wallets) and that dependency vanishes, but now you're the security team, the backup plan and the person who can't ring anyone if it goes wrong.
- **Withdrawals are the only test that doesn't lie.** A platform can look glossy and well while quietly turning the cash-out tap a little tighter every week. Money leaving smoothly, every time you ask, is the clearest sign an exchange is actually healthy.
- **Regulation is a floor, not a hug.** A licence trims some risks. It does not make a platform safe, and anyone telling you it does is selling something.

So when we talk about the best crypto exchanges, we mean the ones with believable custody, honest disclosures, and a boring habit of letting customers walk off with their own money whenever they like.

## What makes a crypto exchange safe

No exchange is without risk, so "safe" here means well-run, honest and hard to topple, not bulletproof. Here's what separates the sturdy from the wobbly:

- **Custody and reserves.** Does it explain how your assets are held and kept separate, and does it publish proof-of-reserves or independent attestations? Or does it just say "trust us" in a nice font?
- **Regulatory standing.** Is it actually registered with the right authority where you live, and is that registration current — not a logo nicked from a website?
- **Withdrawal reliability.** A real history of paying people out, especially when markets are on fire, beats any marketing line ever written.
- **Security practices.** Two-factor authentication, withdrawal allowlists, sensible key storage and a clean incident record all say grown-ups are running the place.
- **Operational transparency.** Clear fees, named humans in charge, a real legal entity, support that replies. Dull as ditchwater. Tells you everything.

A working sense of how [blockchains](/blockchains) actually settle transactions helps too. It's the difference between nodding along to an exchange's claims about speed and fees, and spotting when those claims don't add up.

## Types of crypto exchange

Different models, different headaches. Each trades convenience against custody and counterparty risk in its own way.

### Centralised exchanges (CEX)

The familiar one. A company runs the order book, holds the funds, matches buyers to sellers. Easiest way in, with fiat on-ramps, support and deep liquidity. The catch is custodial risk: your coins live with them, so their solvency and honesty become your problem. This is "not your keys, not your coins" in its purest, most expensive form.

### Decentralised exchanges (DEX)

Here you trade straight from your own wallet through smart contracts, no middleman holding the bag. You keep your keys the whole way through, which kills off one big way to lose everything. In exchange, you pick up smart-contract risk, the chore of managing your own wallet, and a learning curve that bites beginners. If you fancy this route, our rundown of [DeFi platforms](/defi-platforms) lays out how these protocols are built and where the trapdoors are.

### P2P exchanges

Peer-to-peer marketplaces put individual buyers and sellers together, often with the platform just holding the funds in escrow. Handy for flexible payment methods and for regions where banking is patchy. The trade-off: all your risk now sits on the stranger across the table. Reputation scores, escrow and actually confirming the payment landed aren't optional here.

### Brokers

Brokers sell you crypto directly at a quoted price instead of matching you against other traders. Quick, simple, kind to beginners — but that convenience is baked into a wider spread. Check one thing before you commit: can you withdraw to an external wallet, or can you only sell back to them? If it's the latter, you don't really own much; you own an IOU.

## Red flags and withdrawal warning signs

The same patterns turn up across every platform that's gone bad. Treat any of these as a reason to stop, breathe, and verify before another penny goes in:

- Withdrawals that drag, get capped, or suddenly demand extra verification nobody mentioned before.
- Pressure to deposit more before you can withdraw, or "fees" and "taxes" you must pay to "release" your funds. That's not a process. That's a scam, and a tired old one.
- Promises of fixed or outsized returns, "guaranteed" profits, or anything sold to you as a sure thing. Run.
- Faceless operators, no verifiable legal entity, or a registration the regulator's never heard of.
- Reviews where the complaint is, specifically, that people can't get their money out. Believe them.

If a withdrawal stalls, screenshot everything and stop topping up immediately. Our [scam warnings](/warnings) section tracks the tactics these outfits use, and the moves rhyme even when the branding gets a fresh coat of paint. The scammers aren't clever. They're just patient.

## How to choose

Start by matching the model to what you're actually trying to do. Want self-custody? Lean toward a DEX with a wallet you control. Want simplicity and easy fiat access? A regulated CEX or broker might suit, as long as you've made peace with the trade-off. Then, before you trust anyone with real money, run a tiny dry run:

1. Deposit a modest amount.
2. Make a small trade.
3. **Withdraw to an external wallet** and watch it clear.

That third step is the one almost everyone skips, and the one that tells you the most.
