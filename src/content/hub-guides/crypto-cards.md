A crypto card promises something appealingly simple: spend your digital assets, or earn crypto rewards, with the same tap you already use for coffee. The reality is more layered. Behind the headline numbers sit token lock-ups, paid tiers, conversion spreads and custody arrangements that quietly reshape the value on offer. This guide explains, in plain terms, how to compare the best crypto cards on what they actually cost and deliver rather than on the figure printed across the advert.

## How crypto cards work

Most crypto cards are issued on the same payment rails as ordinary bank cards, usually through a Visa or Mastercard partnership. The card itself rarely moves crypto to the merchant. Instead, at the point of sale, the provider converts your crypto to local fiat and settles in pounds, euros or dollars. The shop is paid in normal money; the crypto leg happens behind the scenes.

That conversion step is where much of the economics lives. Each time you spend, the provider applies an exchange rate, and the gap between that rate and the true market rate, the conversion spread, is a cost you may never see itemised. Some cards convert in real time; others let you pre-load fiat or a stablecoin to avoid converting volatile assets at the till.

Two broad questions shape every card:

- **Where does the crypto sit before you spend it?** With the provider (custodial) or in a wallet you control (self-custodial)?
- **When and how is it converted to fiat?** At purchase, on top-up, or only when you choose?

Understanding both helps you read past the marketing. If you are still deciding where to hold the assets that will fund a card, our guides to [crypto exchanges](/crypto-exchanges) and [crypto wallets](/crypto-wallets) cover the custody trade-offs in detail.

## Types of crypto card

The label "crypto card" covers several quite different products. The distinctions matter because they carry different risks around custody, credit and conversion.

### Crypto debit cards

A crypto debit card spends funds you already hold, topped up from a custodial balance or linked account. There is no borrowing: you can only spend what you have loaded or converted. These are the most common consumer crypto cards and the easiest to reason about, because your exposure is limited to the balance on the card and the provider holding it. The main costs to watch are top-up fees, conversion spreads and any monthly or inactivity charges.

### Crypto-backed credit cards

A crypto-backed credit card lets you borrow against crypto you pledge as collateral, spending a credit line rather than your own balance. The appeal is keeping market exposure to your assets while accessing spending power. The catch is that the collateral is volatile: if its value falls, you may face a margin call or liquidation of the pledged assets. Interest, liquidation thresholds and how the provider values your collateral are the decisive details. These products suit experienced users who fully understand collateralised lending, not newcomers.

### Tap-to-pay / self-custodial cards

A newer category lets you pay directly from a wallet you control, with conversion arranged at the moment of payment and no provider holding a standing balance for you. This preserves the "your keys, your coins" principle while still working at ordinary terminals or via a mobile app. The trade-offs shift toward you: wallet security becomes your responsibility, and you should check exactly when conversion happens and what spread applies. ## The catch with "high cashback"

Cashback is the headline that sells crypto cards, and it is where careful reading pays off. A double-digit cashback figure almost never applies to everyone on the free tier with no conditions attached. Typically it depends on one or more of the following:

- **Staking or locking a volatile token.** Many programmes require you to buy and lock the provider's own token to unlock higher rates. You are then exposed to that token's price, which can fall while it is locked, and lock-ups can run for months or years.
- **A paid subscription tier.** The best rates may sit behind a monthly or annual fee. Subtract that fee before comparing the rate to a conventional card.
- **Spending caps and category limits.** Headline rates often apply only up to a monthly ceiling, or only on certain merchant categories, with a lower base rate elsewhere.
- **Rewards paid in a volatile asset.** Cashback paid in crypto is worth whatever that crypto is worth when you receive it and when you sell, which may be more or less than the advertised percentage.

The honest way to assess any offer is to work out the real return after fees and spreads. Take the realistic reward rate for your actual spending, subtract subscription costs and conversion spreads, and account for the cost and risk of any token you must lock. A worked example of how a high advertised rate translates in practice is set out in our [Oobit review on pay-with-crypto and 10% cashback](/blog/oobit-review-pay-with-crypto-10-percent-cashback-2026). We avoid describing any reward as guaranteed: volatile-token rewards and lock-ups make outcomes genuinely uncertain.

## Fees and things to check

Beyond cashback, a handful of details determine whether a card is worth holding. Check each before signing up:

- **Country and currency support.** Confirm the card is available and fully functional in your country, and that it supports your spending currency without surprise foreign-exchange charges.
- **Conversion spread.** Ask what rate is applied when crypto is converted to fiat, and how far it sits from the market mid-rate. This is often the largest hidden cost.
- **Custody.** Establish whether the provider holds your assets or you do, and what protections exist if the provider fails. "Not your keys, not your coins" applies to custodial card balances too.
- **Fee schedule.** Look for monthly fees, top-up and ATM withdrawal charges, inactivity fees and card-issuance costs.
- **Lock-up terms.** If higher tiers require staking, note the lock duration, whether you can exit early, and what happens to rewards if you do.
- **Regulatory standing and support.** A verifiable legal entity, clear terms and responsive support are basic but meaningful signals.
