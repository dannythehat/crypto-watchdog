Tap your phone, buy a flat white, pay with Bitcoin. That's the dream a crypto card sells, and it's a tidy one. The reality has more moving parts: token lock-ups, paid tiers, conversion spreads and custody arrangements that quietly nibble away at what you actually get. This guide cuts through that, so you can compare the best crypto cards on what they really cost and deliver, not the figure splashed across the billboard.

## How crypto cards work

Most crypto cards ride the same payment rails as the debit card in your pocket, usually through a Visa or Mastercard partnership. Here's the bit people miss: the card almost never sends crypto to the shop. At the till, the provider converts your crypto into local money and settles in pounds, euros or dollars. The barista gets paid in normal cash; the crypto leg happens out of sight.

That conversion is where a lot of the money quietly changes hands. The provider applies an exchange rate, and the gap between it and the real market rate is the conversion spread — a cost you might never see written down. Some cards convert on the spot; others let you pre-load fiat or a stablecoin so you're not selling a wobbly asset at the checkout.

Two questions tell you most of what you need to know. Where does the crypto sit before you spend it: with the provider (custodial) or in a wallet you control (self-custodial)? And when is it converted to fiat: at purchase, on top-up, or only when you say so? Answer those and the marketing reads a lot clearer. Still deciding where to park the assets that'll feed your card? ## Types of crypto card

"Crypto card" is a roomy label, covering several very different products, each with its own risks around custody, credit and conversion. Lumping them together is how people get caught out.

### Crypto debit cards

A crypto debit card spends money you already hold, topped up from a custodial balance or a linked account. No borrowing: you spend what you've loaded, not a penny more. These are the most common consumer cards and the easiest to get your head around, because your exposure stops at the balance on the card and the provider holding it. Watch the top-up fees, conversion spreads and any sneaky monthly or inactivity charges.

### Crypto-backed credit cards

A crypto-backed credit card lets you borrow against crypto you pledge as collateral, spending a credit line instead of your own balance. The pitch is seductive: keep your market exposure while unlocking spending power. The catch is that collateral moves. If its value drops, you could face a margin call or watch your pledged assets get liquidated. Interest, liquidation thresholds and how the provider values your collateral are the make-or-break details. This one's for people who already understand collateralised lending, not a starter card for week one.

### Tap-to-pay / self-custodial cards

A newer breed lets you pay straight from a wallet you control, with conversion arranged the moment you pay and nobody holding a standing balance for you. It keeps the "your keys, your coins" spirit alive while still working at ordinary terminals. The trade-off lands on you: wallet security becomes your job, and you'll want to check when conversion happens and what spread comes with it. ## The catch with "high cashback"

Cashback is the shiny lure on every crypto card advert, and it's where careful reading earns its keep. That juicy double-digit figure almost never lands for everyone, on the free tier, with no strings. Usually it depends on one or more of:

- **Staking or locking a volatile token.** Many programmes make you buy and lock the provider's own token to reach the top rates. Now you're exposed to its price, which can slide while it's stuck in a lock-up running for months or even years.
- **A paid subscription tier.** The best rates often hide behind a monthly or annual fee. Subtract it before you compare against a boring high-street card.
- **Spending caps and category limits.** Headline rates often apply only up to a monthly ceiling, or only on certain purchases, with a meeker base rate everywhere else.
- **Rewards paid in a volatile asset.** Cashback paid in crypto is worth whatever that crypto is worth when it lands and when you sell. That might beat the advertised percentage. It might not.

The honest way to size up any offer is to do the maths after fees and spreads: take a realistic reward rate for how you actually spend, knock off subscription costs and spreads, then factor in the cost and risk of any token you must lock. For a worked example of how a flashy rate shakes out in practice, see our [Oobit review on pay-with-crypto and 10% cashback](/blog/oobit-review-pay-with-crypto-10-percent-cashback-2026). No reward here is a sure thing: volatile-token rewards and lock-ups make the outcome genuinely uncertain.

## Fees and things to check

Past the cashback, a short list of details decides whether a card earns its place in your wallet. Run through each before you sign up:

- **Country and currency support.** Make sure the card works where you live and handles your currency without a surprise foreign-exchange charge.
- **Conversion spread.** Ask what rate applies when crypto turns into fiat, and how far it sits from the market mid-rate. Often the biggest hidden cost of the lot.
- **Custody.** Find out whether the provider holds your assets or you do, and what protection exists if it goes under. "Not your keys, not your coins" applies to custodial card balances too.
- **Fee schedule.** Hunt for monthly fees, top-up and ATM withdrawal charges, inactivity fees and card-issuance costs.
- **Lock-up terms.** If higher tiers demand staking, note the lock duration, whether you can bail out early, and what happens to your rewards if you do.
- **Regulatory standing and support.** A verifiable legal entity, clear terms and support that answers when you call. Basic, but telling.

## How we rate crypto cards

We judge crypto cards on evidence, not the number painted across the advert, and we flag it plainly when information is patchy. Our review zeroes in on five things:

- **Real return after costs** — the reward rate once fees, spreads and lock-up exposure are stripped out, not the headline figure.

- **Fee transparency** — whether every charge, spreads included, is laid out clearly.
