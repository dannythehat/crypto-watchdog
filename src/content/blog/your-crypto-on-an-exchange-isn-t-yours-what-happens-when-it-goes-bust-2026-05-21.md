# Your Crypto on an Exchange Isn't Yours: What Happens When It Goes Bust

The collapse of FTX in late 2022 was a brutal lesson for millions of investors, but it was far from the first of its kind and it certainly won't be the last. When you leave your crypto on a centralised exchange, you are trusting a third party with your assets, and if that trust is broken, the consequences are dire. The old adage "not your keys, not your crypto" isn't just a catchy phrase for Bitcoin maximalists; it's a fundamental truth about asset ownership in this volatile market.

## TL;DR

*   When a crypto exchange declares bankruptcy, your assets are frozen. You cannot withdraw your funds.
*   Legally, you are considered an "unsecured creditor." This means the crypto you thought you owned is treated as the exchange's property, and you get in line behind secured lenders, employees, and tax authorities to be repaid.
*   The recovery process can take years, sometimes over a decade, as seen with the Mt. Gox collapse.
*   You will likely only recover a fraction of your holdings' value, often valued at the time of the bankruptcy filing, meaning you miss out on any subsequent market recovery.
*   The only proven way to protect your assets from an exchange collapse is to hold them in a self-custody wallet where you control the private keys.

## The Legal Trap: You're a Lender, Not an Owner

When you deposit pounds or euros into a traditional bank, you are protected up to a certain limit by government schemes like the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) in the UK. If the bank fails, the government steps in to make you whole. In the world of crypto, no such safety net exists.

The moment you transfer your Bitcoin, Ethereum, or any other digital asset to an exchange, you are effectively giving them an interest-free loan. You no longer directly own the assets; you own an IOU from the exchange. Buried deep within the terms of service you scrolled past and clicked "agree" on is almost certainly a clause that gives the platform the right to use, lend, and "commingle" your assets with those of other customers and the company itself.

This legal distinction is critical. When the exchange goes bust, the pot of crypto it holds is not neatly segregated with your name on it. Instead, it all becomes part of the bankruptcy estate. You, the retail customer, are now just another creditor in a very long queue. The courts don't see you as the owner of 1 ETH; they see you as someone to whom the bankrupt company owes 1 ETH. It's a subtle but devastating difference.

## The Bankruptcy Nightmare: A Decade-Long Queue

So, what happens on the day an exchange like FTX, Celsius, or Voyager files for bankruptcy?

First, all activity halts. Withdrawals are frozen instantly. Your funds are trapped, and there is nothing you can do to get them out. The company is now under the control of court-appointed administrators or trustees whose primary job is to tally up the assets and liabilities and figure out a plan to repay creditors.

This is where the creditor hierarchy kicks in.
1.  **Secured Creditors:** These are the big fish—investment firms or lenders who gave the exchange huge loans, often with collateral backing them. They get first dibs on the remaining assets.
2.  **Priority Unsecured Creditors:** This group typically includes employee salaries and tax authorities.
3.  **General Unsecured Creditors:** This is you. You and every other retail customer are lumped into this category, at the very back of the queue.

The administrators will spend months, or more often years, trying to locate and secure all the company's assets. This process is incredibly expensive, with lawyers and consultants billing millions, all of which is paid for out of the same pot of money that should be going back to customers. The infamous Mt. Gox hack happened in 2014, and creditors are *still* waiting for their full distribution a decade later. For users of the now-defunct lender [BlockFi](/reviews/blockfi), the story is painfully similar, with recovery estimates looking bleak.

## The Harsh Reality of a "Haircut"

You will not get 100% of your money back. In almost every major crypto bankruptcy, customers have received only pennies on the pound. This loss is referred to as a "haircut."

To make matters worse, the value of your claim is typically pegged to the date the company filed for bankruptcy. For example, if you held 1 Bitcoin on an exchange that went bust when the price was £15,000, your legal claim is for £15,000. If, during the five years it takes for the case to proceed, Bitcoin's price soars to £80,000, you are not entitled to that gain. You will, at best, receive a percentage of the original £15,000 claim, paid out in cash or crypto equivalent.

This is precisely what happened with Celsius. Customers saw the market recover significantly while their assets were locked in legal limbo, their claims shrinking in relative value every day. It's a brutal financial lesson in the risks of custodial platforms, a lesson that many learned after chasing unsustainable yields promised by platforms that, in hindsight, looked a lot like the infamous [BitConnect](/reviews/bitconnect) Ponzi scheme.

## Are "Proof of Reserves" and Insurance Funds Enough?

In the wake of the FTX scandal, many exchanges rushed to publish "Proof of Reserves" (PoR) audits to reassure customers. While a step towards transparency, PoR is far from a guarantee of safety. A PoR audit is merely a snapshot in time. It shows that an exchange held certain assets on a specific day, but it doesn't prove liabilities, show how those assets are being used, or prevent a rogue executive from gambling them away the next day. A high trust score from a PoR audit is helpful, but it's not a shield.

Similarly, "SAFU" (Secure Asset Fund for Users) funds, like the one popularised by Binance, are a welcome initiative but should be viewed with scepticism. These are self-insurance pools, funded by the exchange itself. While our [Binance review](/reviews/binance) notes its significant size, no one knows if it would be sufficient to cover a "black swan" event that causes a catastrophic bank run or a complete collapse. It’s a buffer, not a bailout.

## Safer Alternatives

The risk of exchange bankruptcy cannot be eliminated, but it can be almost entirely avoided. The solution is to take control of your own assets.

*   **Self-Custody is King:** The most robust way to protect yourself is by using a hardware wallet. Devices like the Ledger or Trezor store your private keys offline, making them immune to exchange hacks, freezes, and bankruptcies. Our [Ledger Nano X review](/reviews/ledger-nano-x) provides a deep dive into how these devices work. This approach places the responsibility for security squarely on your shoulders—you must protect your 24-word seed phrase at all costs—but it means you truly own your crypto.
*   **Use Exchanges as On-Ramps, Not Vaults:** Treat centralised exchanges like a foreign currency exchange booth at the airport. You go there, swap your pounds for euros, and immediately put the euros in your own wallet. Do the same with crypto: buy your assets and withdraw them to your self-custody wallet straight away.
*   **Explore Decentralised Exchanges (DEXs):** Platforms like [Uniswap](/reviews/uniswap) allow you to trade directly from your self-custody wallet. You connect your wallet, make a trade, and the assets are sent back to your wallet, all without a central intermediary holding your funds. Be aware, however, that DeFi has its own risks, including smart contract bugs and exploits. As our regular [scam warnings](/warnings) show, hackers are constantly targeting these protocols.

## Action Checklist

If you have funds on a centralised exchange right now, here is what you should consider doing.

1.  **Audit Your Holdings:** Make a list of every exchange where you hold crypto and how much is on each.
2.  **Read the Terms of Service:** Do a keyword search for "title," "ownership," and "commingle" in the legal documents of your exchange. Understand what rights you have given away.
3.  **Invest in a Hardware Wallet:** Purchase one *directly from the official manufacturer* to avoid tampering.
4.  **Initiate a Test Withdrawal:** Before moving a large amount, send a small test transaction from the exchange to your new hardware wallet to ensure you have the process right.
5.  **Withdraw Your Funds:** Once you are comfortable, move the bulk of your long-term holdings off the exchange and into your own custody. Only leave what you are actively trading and can afford to lose.

The convenience of a centralised exchange is tempting, but the hidden risks are immense. History has shown time and again that when these platforms fail, the customers are the last to be paid and the first to lose everything.
