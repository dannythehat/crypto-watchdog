## Weaponised Adverts: Phishing on Trusted Platforms

For years, the advice has been simple: don't click suspicious links. But what happens when those links appear at the top of a Google search or as a promoted post on X? This is the new frontier of crypto phishing, and it’s proving brutally effective.

Recent reports from our team have highlighted a new script, dubbed the ['MS' Drainer, which has already stolen over $1.1 million](/warnings/new-ms-drainer-steals-over-1-1m-via-google-x-ads-phishing-2026-04-28) by using this very tactic. Scammers identify a trending project—often a new protocol or an anticipated airdrop—and create a convincing replica of its official website. They then buy ad space to ensure their malicious site appears above the real one in search results.

When a user clicks the ad and lands on the fake site, they are prompted to "Connect Wallet" to claim their airdrop or use the protocol. This is where the trap is sprung. Instead of a standard connection request, the user is asked to approve a transaction that grants the scammer's smart contract broad permissions over their assets. This isn't about stealing your seed phrase; it's about getting you to hand over the keys one token at a time. Once approved, the drainer script automatically and rapidly transfers valuable tokens and NFTs out of your wallet.

This method is particularly insidious because it preys on a combination of urgency (fear of missing out on an airdrop) and trust in platforms like Google. The average user doesn't expect the top search result to be a financial landmine.

## The Double-Dip: Exploits Followed by Recovery Scams

The DeFi space remains a high-stakes environment where protocol vulnerabilities can lead to catastrophic losses. This was demonstrated starkly by DeFi protocol Pike Finance, which suffered two major exploits in the space of a week, resulting in losses exceeding $1.9 million. The attackers manipulated a vulnerability in the protocol's smart contracts to drain funds across multiple blockchain networks.

Whilst the initial exploit is damaging enough, it triggers a second wave of scams. Within hours of a hack being publicised, social media and Discord channels are flooded with posts from accounts claiming to be 'blockchain investigators' or 'asset recovery specialists'. These predators target the victims' desperation.

A prime example of this model is the network of operations like the one detailed in our [CryptoRecoveryPro review](/reviews/crypto-recovery-pro). These outfits promise to trace and retrieve stolen funds for an upfront fee or a percentage of the recovered amount. The reality is that, in most cases, once assets are moved through privacy mixers like Tornado Cash, they are effectively gone forever. These 'recovery' services simply take the victim's fee and disappear, compounding the initial loss. It's a cruel tactic that exploits people at their most vulnerable.

## AI Hype: The New Lipstick on an Old Ponzi Pig

Artificial Intelligence is the buzzword of the moment, and scammers have been quick to co-opt its credibility. We are seeing a proliferation of projects that promise impossibly high and stable returns, allegedly generated by sophisticated AI trading bots or yield-farming algorithms.

These schemes are nothing more than modernised Ponzi schemes. They use funds from new investors to pay 'returns' to earlier ones, creating the illusion of a profitable enterprise. The "AI" is pure marketing fiction, a black box designed to discourage questions. A clear case we've flagged is the [YieldMax AI scam](/warnings/yieldmax-ai-scam-warning), which uses all the classic hallmarks: promises of guaranteed daily profits, a multi-level referral system, and a complete lack of transparency about its supposed trading strategy.

This is a direct echo of past disasters. Veterans of the 2017 bull market will remember the spectacular collapse of [BitConnect](/reviews/bitconnect), which used a similar "trading bot" narrative to orchestrate a multi-billion dollar Ponzi scheme. The lesson remains the same: any platform guaranteeing high returns with low risk is almost certainly a scam. The crypto market is inherently volatile; sustainable yield comes from transparent, verifiable on-chain activity, not magic AI bots. The failure of projects like [Anchor Protocol](/reviews/anchor-protocol) on the Terra/Luna network, which collapsed after its unsustainable 20% APY proved to be just that, should serve as a permanent reminder.

## Memecoin Mania: A Factory for Rug Pulls

The allure of turning a few quid into a fortune on a viral memecoin is powerful, but it remains one of the riskiest games in crypto. The recent popularity of Layer 2 networks like Base and high-throughput chains like Solana has made launching new tokens cheaper and faster than ever—dramatically lowering the bar for scammers.

The dominant scam model is the presale rug pull. Developers create a token with a catchy name and meme, generate hype on X and Telegram, and then launch a "presale" where investors can send crypto (usually SOL or ETH) to an address to buy the token before it lists on a decentralised exchange (DEX). The recent [$1 million rug pull of the Condo memecoin on Solana](/warnings/condo-memecoin-on-solana-rug-pulls-for-1m-after-presale-hype-2026-05-02) is a textbook example. After raising a substantial sum from hopeful speculators, the anonymous developers simply walked away with the funds, and the token was never launched.

Assessing the trust score of these projects is nearly impossible. They have no product, no history, and are run by anonymous teams. Participating in a memecoin presale is not investing; it is pure gambling, with the odds heavily stacked in the house's favour.
