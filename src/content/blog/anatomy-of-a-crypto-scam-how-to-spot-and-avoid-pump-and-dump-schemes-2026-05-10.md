# Anatomy of a Crypto Scam: How to Spot and Avoid Pump-and-Dump Schemes

You see it on X, or in a private Telegram channel: a "secret tip" about a new micro-cap token that is about to "go parabolic." The chart looks explosive, testimonials are flooding in, and the fear of missing out is almost physical. Before you swap your Ether for a coin named after a cartoon dog, it is worth understanding the specific mechanism being used against you.

A pump-and-dump is not bad luck, a failed project, or a market that simply turned. It is a coordinated form of market manipulation in which a small group inflates the price of a thinly traded token and then sells into the demand they manufactured. Everyone who buys during the hype is, in the organisers' own language, the *exit liquidity*. This article focuses narrowly on that scheme — how it is built, where it operates, the signals that expose it, and what the law actually says about it.

If you want the broader catalogue of scam types, our guide to [how to spot a crypto scam in 2026](/blog/how-to-spot-crypto-scam-2026) covers the wider landscape. Here we stay on pump-and-dump.

## TL;DR

* **What it is:** A coordinated fraud in which organisers quietly accumulate a low-value token, drive its price up with manufactured hype (the "pump"), then sell their holdings into that demand (the "dump"), collapsing the price.
* **Where it happens:** Low-cap and micro-cap tokens on decentralised exchanges, promoted through Telegram and Discord "signal" or "pump" groups and amplified by paid influencer shilling on X.
* **The hard truth:** If you are *invited* into a pump, you are not an insider. You are the buyer the insiders need in order to sell.
* **Red flags:** Concentrated holder distribution, unlocked liquidity, anonymous teams, countdown-timer "coordinated buys", and language promising fixed multiples like "100x".
* **The law:** Market manipulation is illegal in major jurisdictions. US authorities filed the first criminal charges for crypto market manipulation in 2024, and the UK's FCA treats it as a criminal offence carrying custodial sentences.
* **Protection:** Verify on-chain before you buy, distrust unsolicited tips, size positions so a total loss is survivable, and prefer liquid, established assets over hype-driven micro-caps.

## What a Pump-and-Dump Actually Is

A pump-and-dump is one of the oldest forms of securities fraud. In the penny-stock era, boiler rooms cold-called victims to tout shares in near-worthless companies, sold their own holdings into the buying, and vanished. The playbook in 2026 is identical; only the tooling has changed. Anyone can deploy a token on a decentralised exchange in minutes for a few pounds in gas, with no listing review, no disclosure requirements, and no gatekeeper. That frictionlessness is exactly what makes the scheme so easy to run at scale.

The distinguishing feature of a pump-and-dump, versus other crypto frauds, is that the *price action itself is the product*. There is rarely a real business, roadmap, or user base. The token exists to be inflated and offloaded. That is what separates it from a [rug pull](/blog/anatomy-of-a-rug-pull-7-on-chain-signals-2026), where developers typically drain a liquidity pool or disable selling outright, and from a long-running Ponzi, which pays early participants from later deposits over months or years. A pump-and-dump can begin and end inside a single afternoon.

## The Lifecycle: Four Stages

Most schemes move through four reasonably predictable stages. Knowing them helps you recognise where you are standing before you commit money.

| Stage | What the organisers do | What you see from the outside | The tell |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Accumulation | Quietly buy a large share of a low-liquidity token at the floor, often across several wallets to disguise concentration | Nothing. Volume is flat, the token is unknown | A handful of wallets already hold most of the supply before any hype begins |
| 2. Marketing / shilling | Seed Telegram and Discord groups, pay influencers, deploy bots to fake a "buzzing community", post fabricated partnership or listing rumours | A sudden wall of upbeat posts from new or low-follower accounts; "this is early" messaging | Coordinated, repetitive hype with no verifiable substance behind it |
| 3. The pump | Trigger a "coordinated buy" at a set time; let retail FOMO chase the candle | A near-vertical price spike and urgent "buy now before it's too late" pressure | Price detached from any news, utility, or organic adoption |
| 4. The dump | Sell the accumulated bag into the inflow at the top, often in waves to avoid spooking buyers too early | A violent crash, frequently 80–99%, while organisers go quiet | Top wallets distributing as the price peaks; "hold the line" messaging aimed at trapping you |

The critical insight is in stage four. Because the organisers hold so much of the supply, their selling overwhelms the order book the moment buying momentum fades. The people who bought near the peak cannot all exit, and most do not try until the loss is already severe. The profit was always going to flow from the late buyers to the early accumulators — that is the entire design.

## Where Pump-and-Dumps Live

These schemes cluster in a few predictable places.

**Low-cap and micro-cap tokens.** Manipulation requires a thin market. Moving the price of a token with a few thousand pounds of liquidity takes very little capital; moving a deeply liquid asset takes more money than any small group can muster. This is why pump-and-dumps target obscure tokens and almost never the largest, most-traded assets.

**Telegram and Discord "signal" and "pump" groups.** Some groups are openly branded as pump groups, promising to announce a "coordinated buy" at a set time. They present this as a fair game where everyone profits together. It is not. The administrators and a tier of paying VIP members buy in stages ahead of the public announcement, so by the time the broad membership is told to buy, the early tiers are already selling. The public "signal" is the dump trigger, not the start line.

**Influencer shilling on X and YouTube.** Paid promotion is a core distribution channel. A token with no organic interest can be made to look like a movement when several accounts with large followings post about it within the same window — sometimes disclosed as paid, frequently not. Treat any "tip" from an account that profits from your attention as marketing, not analysis.

The clearest historical illustration remains the SQUID token of late 2021, which piggybacked on the Netflix series *Squid Game*. The price ran up by tens of thousands of percent on pure hype before the anonymous creators drained the project and the token collapsed to effectively zero within minutes, [as CBS News reported at the time](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/squid-game-cryptocurrency-rug-pull-scam/). SQUID combined a manipulated price ramp with code that blocked ordinary holders from selling — a reminder that these categories overlap, and that hype is the common ignition source.

## On-Chain Red Flags: The Blockchain Does Not Lie

Organisers are skilled at manufacturing social proof. What they cannot easily hide is the ledger. Basic on-chain due diligence is the single most effective defence, and it does not require any coding ability.

Find the token's contract address on a data aggregator, then paste it into the relevant block explorer (Etherscan for Ethereum, Solscan or a Solana explorer for Solana, BscScan for BNB Chain). Check three things:

* **Holder distribution.** Open the holders tab. If the top five or ten wallets control a large share of the supply — say 30–50% or more — that concentration is the ammunition for the dump. A small group can crash the price at will. Watch for wallets that are clearly linked or funded from the same source, which disguises true concentration.
* **Liquidity.** A DEX-listed token needs a liquidity pool. Many manipulators create the pool but never lock it, meaning they can withdraw the paired asset at any moment. Unlocked or briefly-locked liquidity is a severe warning. Note that locked liquidity protects against *that specific* exit, not against a coordinated sell of the token itself.
* **The contract.** Is it verified on the explorer? An unverified contract is opaque and may contain functions that mint unlimited supply, blacklist sellers, or set punitive transfer taxes. Free token-scanner tools can flag many of these automatically, though no scanner is a substitute for judgement.

These checks take a few minutes and rule out a large share of obvious schemes. They are the same discipline behind our [Trust Scores](/blog/understanding-trust-scores), which weigh transparency, custody, and on-chain signals rather than marketing claims.

## Social and Behavioural Red Flags

The on-chain data tells you about the token. The messaging tells you about the people. The following pattern is consistent across schemes:

| Red flag | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Fixed-multiple promises ("100x", "guaranteed gains") | Legitimate projects discuss technology and adoption, not predetermined returns. No honest party can promise a price outcome. |
| Manufactured urgency | "Buy now before it's too late" exists to short-circuit your due diligence. Real opportunities survive a night's sleep. |
| Coordinated, repetitive posting | A wall of near-identical praise from new or low-follower accounts is a paid or botted campaign, not organic interest. |
| Anonymous team, no verifiable history | Accountability is impossible when no one can be identified. Anonymity is not always fraud, but it removes your recourse. |
| "We're all in this together" framing in pump groups | Designed to make you hold while insiders sell. The group's interests are not aligned with yours. |
| Vague or plagiarised whitepaper | Buzzwords without a concrete use case signal there is no product beneath the price. |

If two or more of these appear together, the safe assumption is that you are looking at a distribution event aimed at you.

## The Legal Status: This Is Market Manipulation

It is worth being precise: a pump-and-dump is not a grey area or a "degenerate" gamble that participants knowingly accept. It is fraud, and regulators are increasingly treating crypto manipulation the way they treat manipulation in any other market.

In October 2024, US authorities brought what the Department of Justice described as the first criminal charges against financial services firms for market manipulation and wash trading in the crypto industry. The case, which arose from an FBI undercover operation, charged eighteen individuals and entities and seized more than $25 million in cryptocurrency, [according to the DOJ's announcement](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ma/pr/eighteen-individuals-and-entities-charged-international-operation-targeting-widespread). The Securities and Exchange Commission filed parallel civil charges against three so-called market makers and nine individuals for manipulating the prices of crypto assets, [as set out in the SEC's press release](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024-166). Enforcement in this area has continued to expand since.

In the United Kingdom, the position is similarly firm. The Financial Conduct Authority treats market manipulation as a serious offence; its [market abuse guidance](https://www.fca.org.uk/markets/market-abuse) explains that criminal market manipulation can carry custodial sentences and unlimited fines. The FCA is also building a dedicated market-abuse regime for cryptoassets that mirrors the rules already applied to traditional financial instruments, and unauthorised financial promotion of cryptoassets to UK consumers is itself a criminal offence.

The practical takeaway is twofold. First, organising or knowingly promoting a pump-and-dump can expose you to criminal and civil liability — "it's just crypto" is not a defence. Second, because much of this activity is cross-border and pseudonymous, enforcement is slow and recovery for victims is rare. The law is on your side in principle, but it is a poor substitute for not buying in the first place.

## How to Protect Yourself

Defence against pump-and-dumps is mostly about discipline, not technical wizardry.

* **Distrust unsolicited tips by default.** A genuinely early opportunity is not broadcast to a paid group of strangers. If someone is telling you to buy, ask what they gain when you do.
* **Do the on-chain checks first.** Holder concentration, liquidity status, and contract verification take minutes and filter out most schemes before emotion takes over.
* **Never let urgency override process.** The "before it's too late" pressure is engineered. If a token cannot withstand a day of scrutiny, that is your answer.
* **Size for total loss.** If you choose to speculate on a micro-cap despite the risks, commit only what you can afford to lose entirely, and assume the realistic outcome is a loss.
* **Prefer liquid, established assets.** Deeply traded assets cannot be moved by a small group the way a thin micro-cap can. They are not immune to volatility, but they are structurally far harder to manipulate.
* **Use reputable venues and secure custody.** A major exchange's listing process filters out the most obvious scams, though it is not a guarantee — even on a large platform like [Bitget](/reviews/bitget), the responsibility for vetting an individual token remains yours. Keep long-term holdings in self-custody rather than chasing tokens on impulse.
* **Verify, then verify again.** Cross-reference any claimed partnership, listing, or audit against the named third party's own channels. Fabricated announcements are a staple of the marketing stage.

None of this is foolproof, and anyone who tells you a method is "risk-free" is selling something. The goal is not perfect safety; it is removing yourself from the specific trap a pump-and-dump is built to set.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between a pump-and-dump and a rug pull?**
They overlap but are not identical. A pump-and-dump inflates a token's price through coordinated hype and then sells into the demand, leaving late buyers holding a crashed asset. A rug pull is typically the developers draining the liquidity pool or disabling selling so holders cannot exit at all. Many real cases, like SQUID, combine both: a hyped price ramp followed by an exit that traps holders.

**Is a "pump group" a fair way to make money if I get in early?**
No. Public pump groups are structured so that administrators and paying VIP tiers buy ahead of the announcement and sell into the broad membership's buying. By the time you receive the "coordinated buy" signal, the people who organised it are already exiting. You are the demand they need, not a beneficiary.

**Are pump-and-dump schemes illegal?**
Yes, in major jurisdictions market manipulation is unlawful. US authorities filed the first criminal charges for crypto market manipulation in 2024, with parallel SEC civil action, and the UK's FCA treats market manipulation as a criminal offence carrying custodial sentences. Enforcement is real but slow, and victim recovery is rare, which is why prevention matters more than relying on the law afterwards.

**How can I check a token on-chain before buying?**
Find the contract address on a data aggregator, paste it into the chain's block explorer, and review three things: how concentrated the supply is among the top wallets, whether the liquidity pool is locked, and whether the contract is verified. High concentration, unlocked liquidity, or an unverified contract are each serious warnings. Free scanner tools can speed this up, but they do not replace your own judgement.

**Why are memecoins and low-cap tokens so often targeted?**
Manipulation needs a thin market. A token with little liquidity can be moved dramatically with very little money, and a memecoin with no underlying utility has a price driven purely by sentiment — which is easy to manufacture. Large, deeply traded assets require far more capital to move and are correspondingly harder to manipulate.

**If a token is already up 500%, have I missed the dump or is it safe to join?**
A vertical move with no underlying news is more likely the pump in progress than a sustainable trend, which means the dump is the next stage, not a passed danger. Chasing a spike puts you exactly where the scheme wants late buyers. The safer assumption is that a price detached from substance will revert, often violently.

## The Bottom Line

A pump-and-dump survives on two things: a thin market the organisers can move, and your fear of missing out. Remove either and the scheme falls apart. You cannot change how thin a micro-cap's order book is, but you can refuse to be rushed, you can read the ledger before you read the hype, and you can keep the size of any speculation small enough that being wrong is survivable. The blockchain is transparent for a reason — use it before the marketing uses you.
