You get a message on Telegram from a friend, or you see a post from an influencer you follow. There is a new token, a "hidden gem" on a decentralised exchange, and it is supposedly about to do a "100x." The chart is already a wall of green candles, and the fear of missing out (FOMO) starts to creep in.

Before you connect your wallet and swap your savings, it is worth pausing to ask one question: is this a genuine opportunity, or am I being lined up as the exit liquidity for someone else's scheme?

Pump-and-dump schemes are one of the most common forms of crypto market manipulation. They are often organised, coordinated, and built around a single goal: moving money from a crowd of hopeful buyers to a small group of insiders. This guide explains how the scheme works, the warning signs you can check yourself, and a practical checklist to lower your risk. We will not promise you a way to "always win" - that promise is itself a red flag - but we will show you how to spot the patterns before you click buy.

## What a pump-and-dump scheme actually is

A pump-and-dump is a market manipulation tactic that predates crypto by decades. It was a staple of the penny-stock "boiler rooms" of the 20th century, where brokers cold-called investors to inflate thinly traded shares before selling their own holdings.

Crypto has given the old playbook a faster engine. A 24/7 global market, the speed of social media, the low cost of creating a new token, and uneven regulation across jurisdictions combine to make manipulation cheaper and quicker than it ever was in equities.

The mechanics are usually the same three stages:

1. **Accumulation.** A group of insiders quietly buys or mints a large share of a new, low-value token. Many of these tokens are created purely for the scheme and have no real product behind them.
2. **The pump.** The insiders run a coordinated promotion campaign across X, Telegram, Discord, and short-form video. They may pay influencers to "shill" the coin, use bots to fake engagement, and circulate fabricated news about partnerships or exchange listings. This manufactured excitement pulls in retail buyers and drives the price up sharply.
3. **The dump.** Once the price hits a target, insiders sell their large holdings into the wave of new buyers. Because they control so much of the supply, that selling overwhelms demand and the price collapses, often within minutes.

The people who bought during the hype are left holding tokens worth a fraction of what they paid. It is close to a zero-sum transfer: the gains of the organisers come largely from the losses of everyone who arrived late.

### How common is this, really?

This is not a fringe problem. Blockchain analytics firm Chainalysis found that of the millions of tokens launched in 2024, only a small fraction showed signs of sustained, legitimate trading, while a meaningful share displayed pump-and-dump characteristics. In its market-manipulation research, Chainalysis identified roughly **3.6% of tokens launched in 2024 as showing on-chain patterns consistent with pump-and-dump schemes**, and estimated around **$2.57 billion in suspected wash trading** across the chains it studied ([Chainalysis, Crypto Market Manipulation 2025](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/crypto-market-manipulation-wash-trading-pump-and-dump-2025/)).

Regulators are paying attention. According to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center, Americans reported a record **$9.3 billion in losses to cryptocurrency-related crime in 2024**, a sharp rise on the prior year ([FBI / IC3 2024 Annual Report, via the FBI](https://www.fbi.gov/news/press-releases/cryptocurrency-and-ai-scams-bilk-americans-of-billions)). Not all of that is pump-and-dump, but it underlines how much money flows toward crypto fraud generally - and why protective habits matter more than any single "tip."

## The pump phase: hype, FOMO, and manufactured demand

Recognising the pump is your first line of defence. The promotion is engineered to bypass your judgement and trigger an impulsive purchase. Watch for this cluster of behaviour:

- **Urgent, emotive language.** The messaging is rarely calm or analytical. Expect rocket emojis, exclamation marks, and pressure phrases such as "buy now before it is too late" or "the next 1000x." Urgency is a tool to stop you thinking.
- **"Insider" or "leaked" information.** Promoters often claim special knowledge: a "guaranteed" Tier-1 exchange listing, a "secret" partnership, or "whales accumulating quietly." This information is almost always fabricated and impossible to verify.
- **Coordinated influencer waves.** If dozens of unrelated accounts suddenly post about the same obscure token within the same window, treat it as a paid campaign rather than organic interest.
- **Price over product.** The conversation is entirely about the chart and potential gains. There is little or no substantive discussion of what the project does, who built it, or what problem it solves.
- **Guarantees of any kind.** No legitimate investment is "risk-free" or "guaranteed." In regulated markets, that language is itself a sign of fraud.

A useful mental model: legitimate projects sell you a product or a thesis. Pump-and-dumps sell you the price.

## On-chain forensics: what the blockchain reveals

The blockchain is a public ledger, and that transparency is one of the few advantages retail buyers have. You do not need to be a developer to run a few basic checks. Using a block explorer such as Etherscan (Ethereum), BscScan (BNB Chain), Basescan (Base), or Solscan (Solana), look up the token's contract address and review the following.

### 1. Token holder distribution

Open the "Holders" or "Distribution" tab. In a healthy, decentralised project, supply is spread across many thousands of wallets, with no single holder dominating.

In a pump-and-dump setup, you will often see the top handful of wallets holding a very large share of supply. When a small number of addresses control most of the tokens, those holders can crash the price whenever they choose. Concentrated supply is one of the strongest structural warning signs.

### 2. Liquidity and whether it is locked

For a token to trade on a decentralised exchange, it needs a liquidity pool. Two checks matter here:

- **Pool size.** A very small pool means tiny amounts of buying can spike the price, creating a false impression of demand.
- **Locked or not.** If the project's liquidity is not locked or burned, the developers can withdraw it at any time. That is the classic "rug pull," which leaves the pool empty and the token effectively worthless.

### 3. Contract behaviour

Some scam contracts contain malicious functions. A "honeypot" lets you buy but blocks you from selling. Others let the deployer mint unlimited new tokens, change fees to confiscatory levels, or blacklist wallets. Free contract scanners exist, but treat them as a first filter, not a guarantee - a clean scan does not prove a project is safe.

### 4. Token age and trading history

Many manipulated tokens are days or hours old. A token created this morning with a parabolic chart and thousands of holders is far more likely to be manufactured than to reflect genuine adoption.

## A red-flag reference table

Use this table as a quick triage before you ever commit funds. The more rows that apply, the higher the risk.

| Red flag | Where to check | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Top wallets hold most of the supply | Block explorer "Holders" tab | Insiders can dump and crash the price at will |
| Liquidity not locked or burned | Explorer / liquidity locker tools | Developers can pull liquidity (rug pull) |
| Token only days or hours old | Explorer contract creation date | No track record; classic launch-and-dump setup |
| Promises of "guaranteed" or "100x" returns | Social posts, group chats | No legitimate investment is guaranteed |
| Sudden coordinated influencer push | X, Telegram, Discord | Hallmark of a paid, orchestrated pump |
| Anonymous team, no verifiable history | Project site, LinkedIn, GitHub | No accountability if it collapses |
| Vague or plagiarised whitepaper | Project documentation | Signals there is no real product |
| Cannot sell after buying (honeypot) | Contract scanners / test transaction | Funds are trapped by design |
| Pressure to act immediately | Any channel | Urgency is used to stop you doing diligence |
| "Insider" or "leaked" listing news | Social channels | Almost always fabricated |

## Why regulators treat this as fraud

Pump-and-dump and related wash-trading schemes are not legal grey areas in major jurisdictions - they are prosecuted as fraud and market manipulation.

A widely reported example came in October 2024, when the FBI ran an undercover operation, sometimes referred to as "Operation Token Mirrors," in which it created its own token, NexFundAI, to attract manipulators. The operation led the Securities and Exchange Commission to charge several firms describing themselves as market makers - including Gotbit, ZM Quant, and CLS Global - along with multiple individuals, for generating artificial trading volume and manipulating crypto-asset prices ([SEC press release, October 2024](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024-166); reporting via [Fortune](https://fortune.com/crypto/2024/10/09/fbi-doj-crypto-market-manipulation-fraud-charges/)). The Commodity Futures Trading Commission has separately brought charges in digital-asset pump-and-dump matters as well ([CFTC press release](https://www.cftc.gov/PressRoom/PressReleases/8366-21)).

The practical takeaway is not that enforcement will protect you in time - it usually arrives after the money is gone - but that the behaviour you are being asked to join is genuinely illegal, and the people running it know it.

## How to protect yourself: a practical checklist

You cannot eliminate risk in crypto, but you can avoid the most obvious traps. Before you buy any new or unfamiliar token, work through this:

- **Slow down.** Treat urgency as a warning, not an opportunity. If a "chance" disappears because you took an hour to check it, it was not a real opportunity.
- **Run the on-chain checks above.** Holder concentration, liquidity status, contract age, and sell-ability take a few minutes and filter out most scams.
- **Look for a real team and a real product.** A credible, identifiable team, a coherent whitepaper, working code, and a genuine use case are the baseline. Most scam tokens have none of these.
- **Be sceptical of influencers.** Assume anyone promoting an obscure token may have been paid or may already hold a position they intend to sell.
- **Size positions so a total loss would not hurt.** If you choose to experiment with a speculative token, only risk money you can afford to lose entirely.

## What to do if you are already caught in one

If you suspect you have bought into a pump-and-dump:

- **Do not "average down" to recover losses.** Buying more into a collapsing, manipulated token usually deepens the loss.
- **Document everything.** Save the contract address, transaction hashes, screenshots of the promotion, and any chat messages. This record is what investigators and reporting bodies need.
- **Report it.** In the US you can report to the [FBI's IC3](https://www.ic3.gov/) and the [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/tcr); in the UK, to Action Fraud and the Financial Conduct Authority. Reporting will not usually recover your funds, but it can support enforcement and help others.
- **Beware "recovery" scams.** Anyone who contacts you promising to recover your lost crypto for an upfront fee is almost always running a second scam. ## Frequently asked questions

**Is buying a pump-and-dump token illegal?**
Organising or coordinating a pump-and-dump is illegal in major jurisdictions and is prosecuted as fraud and market manipulation. Simply buying a token that turns out to be manipulated is generally not a crime for the buyer, but you can lose your entire stake, and recovery is rare. The safest stance is to avoid tokens showing the red flags above.

**Can I make money from a pump-and-dump if I sell early enough?**
Some people do, but it is closer to gambling than investing, and the odds are stacked against latecomers. Insiders control the timing and the supply; you do not. Anyone selling you a "system" to time these reliably is selling a fantasy, and that pitch is itself a warning sign. We do not recommend trying to trade them.

**How can I tell the difference between a real new project and a pump-and-dump?**
Look for substance over hype: a verifiable team, a clear product and roadmap, working code, locked or burned liquidity, and a wide spread of token holders. Manipulated tokens typically have anonymous teams, concentrated supply, unlocked liquidity, and marketing focused entirely on price. Our guide to [evaluating new token launches](/blog/best-crypto-exchange-uk-2026) and our review section can help you benchmark.

**Are meme coins all pump-and-dumps?**
No, but the category is high-risk and a common vehicle for manipulation. Some meme coins develop real communities and persist; many are launched purely to be pumped and abandoned. Apply the same on-chain checks regardless of how a token is branded.

**What is wash trading and how does it relate to pump-and-dumps?**
Wash trading is when the same party buys and sells an asset to itself (often via bots) to fake trading volume and make a token look popular and liquid. It is frequently used to support the "pump" phase by drawing in real buyers. Chainalysis estimated billions of dollars of suspected wash trading across major chains in 2024.

**Will a hardware wallet protect me from a pump-and-dump?**
A hardware wallet will not stop you from buying a bad token, but it reduces the chance that a malicious contract or leaked seed phrase drains your other holdings. It is a defensive layer, not a screening tool - you still need to do the diligence before you buy.

## The bottom line

Pump-and-dump schemes survive because they exploit emotion: greed, urgency, and the fear of missing out. The defence is unglamorous but effective - slow down, check the chain, distrust guarantees, and keep the bulk of your funds on established platforms and in secure custody.

No checklist can promise you will never be caught out; crypto carries real risk and scams keep evolving. But the patterns in this guide are remarkably consistent across schemes. If a token leans on hype instead of substance, concentrates its supply in a few wallets, and pressures you to act now, the most likely outcome is that you become someone else's exit liquidity. The simplest protective move is often to do nothing at all.
