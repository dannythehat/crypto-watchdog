# When the Exchange Crumbles: Protecting Your Crypto in a Bankruptcy

The crypto market of 2026 is more regulated and more mature than the boom-and-bust years of 2021 and 2022. But one risk has not disappeared: a centralised exchange (CEX) can fail, freeze withdrawals, and enter bankruptcy — and when it does, the coins you thought were "yours" may legally belong to the bankruptcy estate.

This is not a hypothetical. The collapses of FTX, Celsius and Mt. Gox have produced years of court rulings, distribution schedules and recovery figures that we can now look at with hindsight. The picture they paint is sobering, but it is also useful: it tells you exactly where customers stood in the queue, how long they waited, and what they got back.

This guide explains what actually happens to your assets when an exchange goes under, what the major cases teach us, and the concrete steps that reduce your exposure. We do not predict prices, and we do not promise any platform is "safe." We point to the evidence and let you decide.

## The custodial trap: why "your" crypto may not be yours

Most centralised exchanges are **custodial**. When you deposit Bitcoin, Ethereum or a stablecoin, you hand control of the private keys to the exchange. What you hold afterwards is an entry in their internal database — an IOU — not the on-chain asset itself.

People often compare this to a bank deposit. The comparison is misleading in one critical way:

- A UK bank deposit is protected by the **Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)** up to £85,000; a US bank deposit is protected by the **FDIC** up to $250,000.
- Crypto held on an exchange is, in most jurisdictions, **not covered by any equivalent government deposit-insurance scheme.** Some exchanges carry private insurance for hot-wallet hacks, but that is narrow and does not cover insolvency.

So when an exchange becomes insolvent, there is no government backstop to make customers whole. Instead, your claim enters the bankruptcy process — and that process has a strict pecking order.

### What the courts have actually decided

The single most important legal point comes from the Celsius case. In January 2023, Judge Martin Glenn ruled that assets held in Celsius "Earn" accounts were **property of the bankruptcy estate**, not of the customers, because the platform's terms of use transferred title to Celsius. Those customers became **unsecured creditors**.

As law firm Sidley Austin summarised, the court relied on "ordinary contract rules" — the clickwrap terms of use that almost nobody reads ([Sidley Austin analysis of the Celsius ruling](https://www.sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2023/01/celsius-bankruptcy-court-confirms-that-customer-digital-assets-are-property-of-the-estate)).

The lesson is uncomfortable but clear: whether your coins are "yours" in a bankruptcy can come down to the wording of a contract you accepted with a single click. This is the core argument behind [self-custody vs custodial wallets in 2026](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026).

## How an exchange bankruptcy actually unfolds

The exact procedure varies by country, but the pattern across major cases has been remarkably consistent.

1. **Withdrawals are halted.** This is almost always the first public sign of trouble. It is done to stop a "bank run" and preserve remaining assets. By the time withdrawals are frozen, it is usually too late to get your funds out.
2. **A trustee or administrator takes control.** A court appoints an independent party to seize and inventory the remaining assets, including customer funds.
3. **Creditors are classified.** Secured creditors and administrative costs (lawyers, trustees, advisers) typically rank ahead of ordinary customers. Customers usually sit near the bottom as **unsecured creditors**.
4. **Customers file claims.** You must submit a formal proof of claim, with evidence of your holdings, before a deadline. Miss it and you may recover nothing.
5. **Litigation, recovery and distribution.** The estate claws back assets, sells what it can, and distributes the proceeds — often over several years, and frequently valued in fiat at the date of bankruptcy rather than in crypto at today's price.

That last point matters enormously. If you were owed Bitcoin and the estate values your claim at the petition-date dollar price, you do not benefit from any subsequent price rise. You are made a creditor of a fixed dollar amount, not an owner of the coins.

## Case studies: what really happened to customers

The three landmark cases below are the clearest real-world evidence we have. The figures are drawn from court filings and reputable press coverage; recovery rates differ sharply between cases and between customer classes.

### FTX: a high recovery, but only because of how claims were valued

FTX collapsed in November 2022. The U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the District of Delaware confirmed its plan of reorganisation on **7 October 2024**, with Judge John T. Dorsey overruling remaining objections ([CoinDesk: Delaware judge approves FTX plan](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2024/10/07/delaware-judge-approves-ftx-estates-bankruptcy-plan)).

Headlines trumpeted a "119% recovery" for the large majority of creditors. That figure deserves context:

- The estate recovered roughly **$14.7 billion to $16.5 billion** in assets, partly because seized investments (including a stake in the AI firm Anthropic) rose sharply in value after the bankruptcy.
- Crucially, customer claims were valued in **dollars at the November 2022 petition date**, when crypto prices were near a cycle low. Customers who were owed Bitcoin did not receive the much higher 2025–2026 value of that Bitcoin — they received cash based on the depressed 2022 price.

Distributions then rolled out in tranches. According to CoinDesk and other outlets, FTX began repaying creditors in February 2025, followed by multi-billion-dollar rounds through 2025 and into 2026 ([CoinDesk: FTX set to repay $1.6B starting 30 Sep 2025](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/09/19/bankrupt-exchange-ftx-set-to-repay-usd1-6b-to-creditors-starting-on-sep-30)).

The takeaway: even a "successful" bankruptcy took roughly two years to confirm a plan and longer to pay out — and the eye-catching recovery percentage masked the fact that customers missed out on the crypto price recovery.

### Celsius: pennies-on-the-dollar risk made real

Celsius froze withdrawals in June 2022 and filed for Chapter 11. After the January 2023 ruling made Earn assets property of the estate, customers became unsecured creditors.

- The reorganisation plan, approved by around 98% of voting creditors, targeted an eventual recovery in the region of **60–85%** depending on class and asset.
- Distributions began in 2024. CoinDesk reported the plan administrator had paid out over **$2.5 billion** to creditors ([CoinDesk: Celsius pays out over $2.5B](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2024/08/27/celsius-bankruptcy-plan-administrator-pays-out-over-25b)).
- Recovery came partly in liquid crypto and cash, and partly in equity of a new mining company — illiquid, hard-to-value paper rather than spendable funds.

Celsius is the case that turned the phrase "unsecured creditors get pennies on the dollar" from a warning into a documented outcome for many account holders.

### Mt. Gox: a decade of waiting, and still counting

Mt. Gox, once the largest Bitcoin exchange in the world, collapsed in **2014**. Its creditors offer the most extreme lesson on time.

- Repayments in Bitcoin and Bitcoin Cash finally **began in mid-2024**, around a decade after the collapse.
- The repayment deadline has been pushed back repeatedly. CoinDesk reported the trustee continued moving large amounts of Bitcoin in 2026 as a further deadline approached ([CoinDesk: Mt. Gox moves 10,422 BTC as deadline nears, June 2026](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2026/06/02/mt-gox-moves-10-422-bitcoin-worth-usd739-million-to-a-new-wallet-as-deadline-nears)).

Mt. Gox creditors who held on did benefit from receiving actual Bitcoin rather than 2014-priced cash — but only after more than ten years of uncertainty, and many are still waiting.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is my crypto insured if the exchange goes bankrupt?**
Generally no. Government deposit-insurance schemes like the FSCS (UK) and FDIC (US) protect cash in banks, not crypto on exchanges. Some exchanges hold private insurance for specific events such as hot-wallet hacks, but that typically does not cover insolvency. Read the platform's actual policy rather than assuming coverage.

**Do I automatically get my coins back, or just money?**
It depends on the case and how claims are valued. In FTX, claims were valued in US dollars at the November 2022 petition date, so customers received cash based on depressed prices rather than the later, higher crypto value. In Mt. Gox, creditors are receiving Bitcoin and Bitcoin Cash directly. You usually cannot choose, and you may not benefit from any price increase since the bankruptcy.

**How long does it take to get anything back?**
Long. FTX took about two years just to confirm a plan, then began payouts in stages. Celsius started distributions in 2024. Mt. Gox creditors waited roughly a decade for repayments to begin. Plan for years, not weeks, and never count on recovered funds for anything time-sensitive.

**What does "unsecured creditor" mean for me?**
It means you rank near the bottom of the repayment queue, behind secured creditors and the administrative costs of the bankruptcy (lawyers, trustees, advisers). Whether your deposited crypto is treated as yours or as estate property can depend on the platform's terms of use, as the Celsius ruling demonstrated.

**Are decentralised exchanges (DEXs) safer from this risk?**
DEXs and self-custody remove the single-company custodial risk, because you keep control of your keys. But they introduce different risks: smart-contract bugs, user error, scams and irreversible mistakes. They are not a magic fix — see our breakdown in [self-custody vs custodial wallets](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026).

**Should I move everything off exchanges right now?**
Not necessarily. A sensible approach for most people is a split: keep on an exchange only what you actively trade, and move long-term holdings to self-custody such as a hardware wallet. The right balance depends on your amount, skills and comfort with managing your own keys.

**How do I know if an exchange is in trouble?**
Watch for halted or limited withdrawals, sudden unexplained downtime, slow or absent support, and credible (not anecdotal) solvency concerns. By the time withdrawals are frozen it is usually too late, so act on early signals rather than waiting for confirmation.

## The bottom line

Exchange bankruptcies are no longer a theory — they are a body of case law. The pattern is consistent: withdrawals freeze, customers become unsecured creditors, recovery takes years, and what you get back may be valued in fiat at the worst possible moment.

You cannot control whether an exchange fails. You can control how much of your wealth is sitting on it when it does. Keep trading balances small, read the terms, choose better-governed venues, and move long-term holdings into self-custody where the keys — and the risk — are yours.
