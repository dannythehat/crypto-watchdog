# Can't Get Your Crypto Out? A Guide to Withdrawal Nightmares

There are few feelings in crypto more stomach-churning than a withdrawal stuck on "pending." You followed the steps, the funds left your account balance, but they have not arrived in your wallet. Is it a routine technical hold, a compliance check — or the first sign that the platform you trusted is quietly insolvent?

Getting money *into* a crypto platform is almost always frictionless. Getting it *out* is the real test of integrity. This is where promises meet reality and where shaky foundations are exposed. The difficulty is that legitimate delays and fraudulent ones can look identical in the first hours. A genuine anti-money-laundering review and the opening move of an exit scam both start with the same message: "Your withdrawal is being processed."

This guide is about telling them apart. We will cover the legitimate reasons a withdrawal gets blocked, the specific red flags that point to insolvency or fraud, and a practical playbook for what to check, how to document it, and where to report it in the UK. One pattern deserves naming up front because it is unambiguous: **if a platform demands that you pay a fee, "tax," or "deposit" *before* it will release your own funds, that is a textbook advance-fee scam.** We will explain why later, but keep it in mind throughout.

## TL;DR

*   **Some delays are normal.** KYC/AML reviews, security cool-downs after a password change, network congestion, and scheduled maintenance all legitimately hold withdrawals — usually with a clear timeframe and a confirmable reason.
*   **Some delays are fatal.** A platform-wide freeze blamed vaguely on an "upgrade," shifting explanations, or pressure to deposit more to "unlock" your balance are red flags of insolvency or a scam.
*   **Pay-to-withdraw is always a scam.** A real exchange deducts fees *from* your withdrawal. It never asks you to send fresh money first. The UK's Financial Conduct Authority lists this "pay a fee to release funds" pattern as a hallmark of fraud.
*   **Document everything immediately.** Screenshots of balances, transaction IDs, chat logs, and the platform's own terms are the evidence you will need to report and, occasionally, recover.
*   **Report it.** In the UK, report to Action Fraud and to the FCA. Reporting will not usually get your money back, but it protects others and builds the case against the operator.

## First, rule out the boring explanations

Before assuming the worst, rule out the mundane causes. In many cases the problem is a simple, if costly, mistake on the user side rather than anything the platform did.

**Wrong address or wrong network.** This is the crypto equivalent of posting a parcel to the wrong postcode and sealing it shut. Sending a token on the BNB Smart Chain (BEP-20) to an address that only supports Ethereum (ERC-20) will usually result in permanent loss, even though both use the same address format. Many platforms list several networks for the same asset, and picking the wrong one is one of the most common irreversible errors. A small test transaction first — a few pounds' worth — is the single cheapest insurance policy in crypto.

**Network congestion.** Blockchains get busy. During market surges or popular launches, the "gas" fees needed to process a transaction spike, and low-fee transactions can sit unconfirmed for hours. You can check the real status of any on-chain transaction yourself using a block explorer (such as Etherscan for Ethereum) and your transaction ID, or TxID. If the explorer shows the transaction confirmed, the funds have left the platform and the problem is at the receiving end. If there is no transaction at all, the platform has not actually sent anything yet.

**Missing memo or destination tag.** Some assets — XRP, and assets on certain exchanges — require a memo or destination tag, a reference number that tells the receiving exchange which account to credit. Omit it on a deposit and your funds land in the exchange's pooled wallet rather than your account. Support can sometimes recover this, but it is slow and stressful.

If your TxID checks out and the funds are confirmed on-chain, you are almost certainly dealing with a user-side or receiving-platform issue, not a fraud. If the platform refuses to produce a TxID at all, that absence is itself a signal — keep reading.

## Legitimate platform holds: KYC, limits, and maintenance

Sometimes the barrier is a deliberate policy, not a technical fault. These holds are legitimate, even when they are infuriating.

**Know Your Customer (KYC) and Anti-Money Laundering (AML) reviews.** Regulated exchanges are legally required to verify identity and, for larger sums, to check the source of funds. You can often deposit and trade with light verification, then hit a wall the first time you try to withdraw a meaningful amount. A genuine review has recognisable features: it is triggered by a specific, plausible event (a large withdrawal, a new device, a flagged counterparty); it asks for standard documents (government ID, proof of address, source-of-funds evidence); and it comes with a stated process and timeframe. It is annoying, but it is not a trap.

**Security cool-downs.** After you change a password, add a new withdrawal address, or reset two-factor authentication, most reputable platforms impose a withdrawal hold of 24–72 hours. This is a deliberate anti-theft measure: if an attacker compromises your account, the delay gives you time to react. A cool-down that matches the platform's published security policy is a good sign, not a bad one.

**Withdrawal limits.** Daily, weekly, or tiered limits are standard. They are usually a security and compliance measure. The warning sign is not the existence of limits but a *sudden, unannounced reduction* in them — more on that below.

**Scheduled maintenance.** Exchanges do take wallets offline to upgrade infrastructure or integrate new chains. Legitimate maintenance is announced *in advance*, scoped to specific assets, and given an end time. The status page updates. Withdrawals resume when it says they will.

The common thread across all legitimate holds: a **specific reason**, a **defined process**, and a **finite timeframe**. When any of those three is missing, your suspicion should rise.

## The red flags: insolvency and exit scams

This is the worst case. A platform that was functioning normally suddenly slows or suspends withdrawals. The official line is "unscheduled maintenance," a "system upgrade," or "addressing technical issues." Sometimes that is true. Often it is the first public crack in an insolvent or fraudulent operation.

The 2022 collapses followed a recognisable script. On 12 June 2022, the lender Celsius Network paused all withdrawals, swaps, and transfers, locking roughly 1.7 million users out of around $4.7 billion in assets; it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy a month later, as documented in contemporaneous reporting ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/10/06/celsius-executives-withdrew-millions-before-freeze-on-customer-funds.html)). FTX, Voyager, and others ran a similar arc: slow withdrawals, app outages, reassuring statements, then a freeze and a bankruptcy filing. In each case the firms were not holding customer assets in custody as users assumed; they were lending or trading them. When those bets soured, the money was simply not there.

Outright scams are designed to trap funds from day one. They advertise impossibly high, "guaranteed" returns (a phrase no legitimate platform should ever use), accept deposits eagerly, and then engineer reasons you cannot withdraw. The most common and most diagnostic of these is the **advance-fee** play.

Here is the pattern. Your dashboard shows a large, growing balance — often fictitious. You request a withdrawal. Suddenly there is a "withholding tax," a "liquidity fee," an "anti-money-laundering deposit," or an "account upgrade fee" you must *pay in* before the withdrawal can be released. Pay it, and a new fee appears. Then another. The goalposts keep moving because the balance was never real and the only genuine transactions are the ones flowing *to* the scammer.

**No legitimate exchange ever works this way.** A real platform deducts its fee *from* the amount you withdraw — it does not ask you to send fresh money to access funds you already hold. The FCA explicitly describes being asked to pay an upfront charge to release funds as a fraud hallmark, both in its [crypto investment scams](https://www.fca.org.uk/consumers/crypto-investment-scams) guidance and in its broader [loan and advance fee fraud](https://www.fca.org.uk/consumers/loan-fee-fraud) warnings. If you take one thing from this article, take this: **the moment you are asked to pay to withdraw, stop. Do not send anything. You are being scammed.**

### Legitimate delay vs scam signal

| What's happening | Likely legitimate | Likely a red flag |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **The reason given** | Specific and verifiable (KYC review, security cool-down, named chain maintenance) | Vague ("system upgrade", "technical issues") with no detail or end date |
| **Timeframe** | A clear, finite window you can hold them to | Open-ended, repeatedly extended, or "soon" |
| **Who is affected** | Just you (account-specific check) or one named asset | Everyone, all assets, all at once |
| **What they ask of you** | Standard ID / source-of-funds documents | Money — a "tax", "fee", or "deposit" to unlock your own funds |
| **On-chain evidence** | A real TxID you can verify on a block explorer | No TxID; balance is only a number on their dashboard |
| **Communication** | Consistent, on the record, via official channels | Shifting stories; pressure; contact moves to private chat |
| **Limits** | Published in the terms you agreed to | Slashed suddenly and without notice |

No single row is proof on its own. A genuine KYC hold can feel vague and open-ended when support is overwhelmed. But when several columns on the right light up at once — especially a demand for payment plus the absence of any on-chain TxID — treat the funds as at high risk and act accordingly.

## Choosing platforms less likely to trap your funds

The best time to avoid a withdrawal nightmare is before you deposit. A few checks tilt the odds:

*   **Check regulatory standing.** In the UK, firms marketing crypto must comply with FCA financial-promotion rules, and many must be registered for anti-money-laundering supervision. Registration is not a safety guarantee, but its complete absence on a platform soliciting UK customers is a meaningful negative. Our roundup of the [best crypto exchanges in the UK for 2026](/blog/best-crypto-exchange-uk-2026) walks through who is registered and what that does and does not cover.
*   **Read the withdrawal terms before funding.** What are the limits? What documents can they demand, and when? Is there a cool-down? Platforms that are upfront about this tend to behave predictably later.
*   **Prefer track record and liquidity.** No exchange is risk-free, but a long-established venue with deep liquidity and transparent operations is generally less likely to suddenly suspend withdrawals than an unknown one promising outsized yields. Our [Kraken review](/reviews/kraken) is an example of the kind of operational due diligence worth doing before you trust a platform with funds.
*   **Use independent risk signals, not just marketing.** We score platforms on observable risk indicators rather than promises; our explainer on [how trust scores work](/blog/understanding-trust-scores) describes the methodology so you can weigh it yourself.
*   **Minimise what you leave on any platform.** The most durable protection is not picking the perfect exchange — it is not leaving more on *any* exchange than you need to. Crypto held in your own wallet cannot be frozen by a third party's "upgrade." Our guide to [self-custody vs custodial wallets](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026) covers the trade-offs, because self-custody shifts the risks onto you (lose your keys, lose your coins) rather than eliminating them.

None of these is a silver bullet, and we would be wary of anyone who claimed otherwise. They are about stacking small advantages so that, if something does go wrong, you are exposed for less and positioned to act faster.

## The playbook: what to do when a withdrawal is stuck

Work through this in order. The early steps are cheap and often resolve the problem; the later ones matter most if it turns out to be fraud.

1.  **Verify on-chain.** Find the TxID and check it on the relevant block explorer. Confirmed on-chain but not in your wallet means the issue is at the receiving end (wrong address, missing memo, or your own wallet). No TxID at all means the platform has not actually sent anything.
2.  **Read the platform's own status page and terms.** Is there an announced maintenance window? A documented cool-down after your recent account change? A stated KYC threshold you just crossed? Matching the delay to a published policy is reassuring; finding no policy that fits is not.
3.  **Document everything now, not later.** Screenshot your balance, the pending withdrawal, every support message, and the relevant terms. Save the platform's URL, your account ID, deposit transaction IDs, and any wallet addresses you sent funds to. If the site later goes dark, this evidence is all you will have. Store it outside the platform — your own device or email.
4.  **Contact support through official channels only.** Use the email or in-app support listed on the platform's verified domain. Be persistent but factual, and reference your TxIDs. Never move the conversation to a private chat opened by someone who messaged *you* first.
5.  **Apply the one hard rule.** If at any point you are asked to pay a fee, tax, or deposit to release your funds, stop immediately and send nothing. There is no legitimate version of this request.
6.  **If it is a platform-wide freeze, treat funds as at risk.** Do not deposit more "to qualify for withdrawal" or "to cover the upgrade." Preserve your evidence and move to reporting.

## How to report a crypto withdrawal scam in the UK

Reporting rarely recovers funds, and we will not pretend otherwise — but it disrupts operators, supports investigations, and protects the next person. Be especially wary here: victims of crypto fraud are aggressively targeted by "recovery experts" who promise to claw back losses for an upfront fee. That is the *same* advance-fee scam wearing a different hat. The FCA reported almost 5,000 fake-FCA scam reports in the first half of 2025, many involving fraudsters claiming to have "recovered" funds from a wallet supposedly opened in the victim's name ([FCA press release](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/press-releases/fake-fca-scams-reported-6-months-2025)). No legitimate body charges an upfront fee to return your money.

Where to report in the UK:

*   **Action Fraud** — the national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. Report online or by phone, with your documented evidence to hand. See the Action Fraud [cryptocurrency fraud](https://www.actionfraud.police.uk/a-z-of-fraud/cryptocurrency) guidance for what to include. (In Scotland, report to Police Scotland on 101.)
*   **The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)** — report scam firms and clone websites through the FCA's [crypto investment scams](https://www.fca.org.uk/consumers/crypto-investment-scams) channel and check the FCA Warning List before trusting any platform.
*   **Your bank or card provider** — contact them immediately if you funded the platform via bank transfer or card. They may be able to halt a pending transfer or open a chargeback, and UK reimbursement rules for some authorised-push-payment fraud may apply.

Move quickly. The sooner a fraudulent transfer is flagged, the better the (still uncertain) odds that some of it can be frozen before it is moved on.

## Frequently asked questions

**Why is my crypto withdrawal blocked when I could deposit fine?**
Depositing is easy by design; withdrawing is where checks and risks concentrate. The benign causes are a KYC/AML review, a post-change security cool-down, a withdrawal limit, or scheduled maintenance — each with a specific reason and timeframe. The concerning causes are insolvency or fraud, which tend to show a vague reason, an open-ended delay, and pressure to pay or deposit more. Check whether the platform can point to a published policy that fits your situation.

**Is being asked to pay a fee or tax to release my withdrawal ever legitimate?**
No. A real exchange deducts its fees *from* the amount you withdraw; it never asks you to send fresh money first. Being told to pay a "tax," "unlock fee," or "deposit" before you can access your own balance is a classic advance-fee scam, and the FCA names this pattern as a fraud hallmark. Stop and send nothing.

**How long should a normal crypto withdrawal take?**
On-chain transfers usually settle within minutes to a couple of hours once broadcast, depending on the network and fee. Legitimate holds — KYC reviews, security cool-downs — are typically measured in hours to a few days and come with a stated reason. A delay that stretches indefinitely with no clear explanation, or that applies to every user at once, is a different matter.

**How can I tell a real maintenance pause from an exit scam?**
Legitimate maintenance is announced in advance, scoped to named assets, and has an end time, with a status page that updates. An exit scam tends to be sudden, platform-wide, vaguely justified ("upgrade", "technical issues"), open-ended, and accompanied by shifting stories or requests for money. The presence or absence of a real, verifiable on-chain TxID is often the clearest tell.

**Where do I report a crypto platform that won't let me withdraw in the UK?**
Report to Action Fraud (online or by phone; Police Scotland on 101 in Scotland) and to the FCA, and contact your bank or card provider straight away if you funded the account by transfer or card. Bring your documented evidence: balances, TxIDs, chat logs, and the platform's terms.

**Can a "recovery service" get my lost crypto back?**
Treat any firm that contacts you promising to recover lost funds for an upfront fee as a scam — it is the same advance-fee fraud aimed at people who have already been hurt once. No legitimate organisation, including the FCA, charges you in advance to return your own money. Report the approach rather than engaging with it.

## The bottom line

The ability to withdraw funds on demand is the most basic service a crypto platform can offer, and a stuck withdrawal is not automatically a disaster — most are KYC holds, congestion, or maintenance that resolve on their own. But the cost of mistaking a fraud for a glitch is total, so the safe move is to verify the on-chain facts, match the delay to a published policy, document everything, and apply one non-negotiable rule: never pay to withdraw. If a platform fails this simple test, nothing else it promises matters.
