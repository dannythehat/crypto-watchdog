## How the low-float market-maker pattern works

Whatever the investigations ultimately conclude about RAVE specifically, the **mechanics** of this kind of collapse are well documented and worth understanding on their own. They explain why "trending" low-cap tokens can move so violently in both directions.

The pattern has four moving parts.

### 1. Concentrated supply and a tiny float

"Float" is the amount of a token actually available to trade. If insiders hold most of the supply and only a small slice circulates on exchanges, the market is **thin**. As [TrendSpider's learning materials on pump-and-dump schemes](https://trendspider.com/learning-center/pump-and-dump-scams-on-low-float-stocks/) explain for low-float assets generally, when very little is freely trading, **even modest buying can produce outsized price moves**.

- Less liquid supply means each buy order moves price further.
- A few hundred thousand dollars can shift a thin market by multiples.
- Charts look "parabolic" not because demand is real, but because there is almost nothing to absorb it.

### 2. Manufacturing the squeeze

A **short squeeze** happens when traders who bet on a falling price are forced to buy back the asset to close their positions, pushing the price even higher. The basics are described clearly in [Bitget's explainer on short squeezes](https://www.bitget.com/wiki/how-short-a-stock).

In a thin token, this becomes a weapon:

- Large holders can make it **look** like a dump is coming (visible sell pressure), baiting traders into shorting.
- With shorts crowded in, a relatively small amount of buying pushes price through **liquidation clusters**.
- Each forced liquidation triggers market buys, which trigger the next cluster — a cascade **upward**.

The result is a stair-step rally that feels unstoppable for a day or two.

### 3. The exit

At the top, concentrated holders have the only meaningful supply — and almost no one to sell to without crashing the price. Selling into thin liquidity is exactly how a "stair-step collapse" begins, the pattern the [ChainCatcher analysis of the RAVE event](https://www.chaincatcher.com/en/article/2259769) describes as explosive growth, frantic squeezes, then a near-total wipeout in a very short window.

- Depth disappears from the order book.
- Late buyers become the **exit liquidity**.
- Liquidations now cascade **downward**, mirroring the way up.

### 4. The silence

In contested episodes, the public-relations playbook is often the same: deny, deflect to "market conditions" or "third-party market makers," and avoid the specific on-chain questions. That is why **independent on-chain verification matters more than any statement** a project issues.

## Why a major-exchange listing is not a safety stamp

A lot of the RAVE damage flowed from a single, understandable assumption: *it is on a big exchange, so it must be safe.*

It is worth being precise about what a listing does and does not mean:

- A listing is a **distribution channel**. It makes a token easy to buy.
- Listing review focuses on the **project and its paperwork**, not on protecting you from a coordinated trading scheme that unfolds after listing.
- An exchange opening an **investigation after the fact** — as Binance, Bitget and Gate.io did here — is the system reacting to damage, not preventing it.

This is not an argument against using reputable exchanges. These are general checks that apply to any low-cap token. Several of them map directly onto the concerns raised about RAVE. None of these tools or sites are affiliated with us, and none guarantee safety — they simply surface risk.

| Check | Where to look | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| Holder concentration | Block explorer "Holders" tab (e.g. Etherscan, BscScan, Solscan) | A few non-exchange wallets hold a large share of supply |
| Liquidity depth | DEX analytics dashboards (e.g. DEXScreener, GeckoTerminal) | Thin depth relative to a very large market cap |

| Team identity | Project site, public profiles | Anonymous team or stock-photo bios |
| Token unlocks | Unlock trackers | Large cliff or unlock arriving soon |
| Perp funding rate | Derivatives data dashboards | Extreme funding can signal a crowded, squeeze-prone setup |
| Insider flow | On-chain analytics | Large holders moving tokens to exchanges before a move |

If several of these flash red at once, the cautious read is simple: **the favourable trade belongs to the insiders, and a late buyer is the exit.** When in doubt, the safest position size on an unknown low-cap is one you can afford to lose entirely — or zero.

## Practical ways to protect yourself

You cannot stop manipulation from happening. You can control your exposure to it.

1. **Size for the risk, not the hype.** A token that is a week old with a tiny float is not a blue chip. Position accordingly.
2. **Be skeptical of vertical charts.** A move that looks too clean is often engineered to look that way.
3. **Read "everyone is short" as a warning, not an entry.** Extreme one-sided positioning is the fuel a squeeze needs.
4. **Verify on-chain before you trust a narrative.** Statements are cheap; transaction history is not.
5. **Mind where your assets live.** If you do hold volatile tokens, understand the trade-off between exchange custody and self-custody in our [self-custody vs custodial wallets guide](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026), and keep long-term holdings off hot wallets — our [best hardware wallet 2026: Ledger vs Trezor comparison](/blog/best-hardware-wallet-2026-ledger-vs-trezor) walks through the options.

If you genuinely need cold storage for assets you intend to hold, a reputable hardware wallet such as a [Ledger](/go/ledger) or [Trezor](/go/trezor) device keeps your keys offline. That protects you from drainers and phishing — it does **not** protect you from buying a manipulated token, which is a separate risk entirely.

**What is a low-float market-maker scheme?**
It is a pattern where most of a token's supply is held by insiders, leaving a tiny tradable float. Because the market is thin, modest buying can spike the price and trigger short squeezes; insiders can then sell into the resulting demand, collapsing the price. The general mechanics are explained in resources like [Bitget's short-selling explainer](https://www.bitget.com/wiki/how-short-a-stock) and [TrendSpider's pump-and-dump overview](https://trendspider.com/learning-center/pump-and-dump-scams-on-low-float-stocks/).

**How can I check a token for these red flags myself?**
Use a block explorer to view holder concentration, a DEX analytics dashboard for liquidity and pair age, an unlock tracker for upcoming supply, and derivatives data for funding rates. If several signals look unhealthy at once, treat the token as high-risk and size accordingly — or stay out.

**What should I do if I lost money on a token like RAVE?**
Be wary of anyone promising to "recover" your funds for a fee — recovery scams target victims of earlier losses. Document your transactions, report to the relevant exchange and authorities, and never pay an upfront fee to a stranger claiming they can retrieve your crypto.
