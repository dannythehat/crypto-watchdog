## What you need before reviewing a crypto platform

Before you start, get a few things straight. Reviewing a platform without the right materials is a bit like walking into a contract negotiation having skipped the terms. You want structure, sources you can verify, and a clear sense of what real evidence looks like, so you're not making judgements on vibes.

A workable review method, like the one in this [crypto risks guide](https://www.okx.com/en-us/learn/crypto/crypto-risks-guide), pulls together three things: exchange security auditing practices, proof or verification documentation, and operational risk controls. The takeaway is simple. A number on an aggregator site isn't enough on its own. You need to trace where that number comes from before you lean on it.

Here are the resources to have open before you begin:

| Resource | Purpose | Where to find it |
|---|---|---|
| Proof-of-Reserves (PoR) reports | Verify platform solvency | Exchange's dedicated PoR page |
| Third-party audit certificates | Confirm independent security testing | Auditor's public report page |
| Regulatory registration records | Cross-check licensing claims | National regulator databases |
| Merkle-tree inclusion tools | Personally verify your funds are included | Exchange or open-source tools |
| Incident history databases | Assess past security failures | Blockchain data providers |

Before you touch any of those, it helps to be clear on what [crypto platform transparency](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/demystifying-crypto-platform-transparency-for-safer-trading) actually means. It isn't just whether a platform has published a white paper. It's whether the platform can show you, in a way you can verify, that what it claims is genuinely true. Those are very different things, and a lot of platforms blur the line on purpose.

A few warnings worth absorbing before you start any review:

- **Never trust a platform that refuses to publish proof of reserves or incident reports.** Silence on these isn't neutral. It's a red flag.
- **Be wary when the only "evidence" of security is a self-assigned score** with no published method behind it. A score with no workings shown is an opinion, not a measurement.
- **Don't confuse trading volume with trustworthiness.** High liquidity has happily sat alongside catastrophic security failures more than once.
- **Treat anonymous or unverifiable team members as a structural risk**, not a cosmetic detail. Who's accountable matters.

It's also worth getting your head around [understanding trust scores](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/understanding-trust-scores) first, because knowing what a score should contain makes it much easier to spot one that's been manufactured rather than measured.

> **Safety warning:** Never trust platforms that refuse to publish proof-of-reserves documentation or incident reports. Legitimate platforms have nothing to hide and actively invite independent scrutiny. Absence of verifiable evidence is itself evidence of risk.

OKX, for instance, [publishes detailed PoR reports](https://okx.com/en-us/proof-of-reserves/download) with downloadable artefacts and user-level inclusion verification tools. That's the standard every platform should be measured against, because it gives you something you can actually test. When a platform can't or won't match that openness, raise your guard accordingly.

**Pro Tip:** Bookmark the PoR or audit verification page of any platform you use regularly. When a new report lands (usually monthly or quarterly), you can confirm your own inclusion in seconds instead of hunting for the page later, possibly under stress.

## Step-by-step: How to conduct a crypto platform review

With your tools and warnings in place, here's the method we use. The steps below mirror the process we run internally at Crypto Watchdog, adapted for an individual trader doing their own due diligence. Work through them in order. Skipping the dull ones is usually where people come unstuck.

**1. Start with the regulatory layer.** Before anything technical, check whether the exchange actually holds licences in the jurisdictions it claims to serve. Cross-reference those claims against the relevant national regulator's public register. Unverifiable or out-of-date licences are an immediate concern, not a paperwork nitpick.

**2. Examine the security architecture.** Look for evidence of cold storage practices (ideally 90% or more of funds held offline), multi-signature wallet controls, and documented incident-response procedures. A platform that has never published a security report, even a short one, is a platform with something to keep quiet about.

![Man examining crypto platform security setup](https://csuxjmfbwmkxiegfpljm.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/blog-images/organization-27129/1778402657789_Man-examining-crypto-platform-security-setup.jpeg)

**3. Verify KYC and AML practices.** The [CoinDesk Exchange Benchmark](https://www.coindesk.com/research/2025/03/18/exchange-benchmark-november-2024) assesses 81 spot exchanges across more than 100 metrics and documents wide variation in KYC policy across the sector. Use a benchmark like this as your reference point when you're judging whether a platform's identity-verification requirements meet a reasonable professional standard.

**4. Test withdrawal functionality.** Plenty of reviewers skip this one. We don't. A platform can take deposits smoothly and then create friction or delays the moment you try to withdraw. Move a small amount out, record the timing and any fees, and compare what happens against the published terms. The gap between the two is often the whole story.

**5. Audit the proof-of-reserves documentation.** Don't just note that a PoR report exists. Download it. Check the timestamp. Confirm the auditor is a recognised firm rather than an anonymous entity. Then use any inclusion tool provided to verify that your own balance actually appears inside the Merkle-tree structure.

**6. Score incident history.** The Trusted Exchange Framework sets out how past incidents should be scored by recency and value lost, and it weights trustworthiness categories according to exchange type. A spot exchange and a derivatives exchange aren't assessed the same way, because their risk profiles differ.

**7. Evaluate customer support quality.** Submit a test query. Note the response time, whether the answer is accurate, and whether the person replying actually understands the platform's policies. A vague or scripted reply tells you something useful about how you'd be treated on a bad day.

**8. Review the team's verifiability.** Are the founders and key people named, with professional histories you can trace? Can you independently confirm the credentials they claim? Anonymous leadership in a regulated financial service is a structural risk, not a stylistic choice.

Here's how three common review approaches stack up on reliability:

| Approach | Transparency | Verifiability | Usefulness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Marketing claims | Low | None | Misleading |
| Third-party benchmarks | High | Strong | Very useful |
| Verifiable audit trails | Very high | Complete | Most reliable |

When you put a [safe crypto exchange checklist](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/how-to-pick-safe-crypto-exchange-2026) into practice, apply these criteria to every platform, not just the ones you already doubt. The most painful losses tend to happen on platforms that looked perfectly credible at first glance.

![Infographic showing five safe crypto review steps](https://csuxjmfbwmkxiegfpljm.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/blog-images/organization-27129/1778403424945_Infographic-showing-five-safe-crypto-review-steps.jpeg)

**Pro Tip:** When reviewing proof-of-reserves, look specifically for Merkle-tree verification rather than a plain attestation letter. A Merkle-tree inclusion check lets you confirm that your specific balance is counted inside the total reserves. That's a different and stronger thing than a platform simply asserting reserves exist somewhere.

It also helps to see [platform trust and security](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/why-crypto-platform-trust-is-key-to-your-security) as two linked questions, not one. A platform can have strong security architecture and still withhold proof that it's operating honestly. You have to weigh both together, because either one failing is enough to hurt you.

## Verification: Ensuring the review's credibility (and your safety)

Once you've worked through the steps, there's one more job: make sure the process and the evidence themselves can be trusted. A completed checklist is only worth something if the sources you used and the proofs you examined are legitimate. Cross-verification is the last layer, and it isn't optional.

The Trusted Exchange Framework is blunt about this. Prefer sources that expose their mechanics, including scoring categories, weightings and how they treat incidents, and that let you verify through PoR files, inclusion checks and named audit partnerships. If a review source can't tell you how it reached its conclusion, it can't be trusted as an input to yours.

Here's the short version of what good looks like versus what should worry you.

**Credibility hallmarks:**

- Published methodology with category weights and scoring formulas
- Downloadable PoR files with user-level inclusion tools
- Named, independently verifiable auditors
- Historical incident disclosures with post-mortem reports
- Regulatory filing cross-references

**Red flags:**

- Trust scores with no published methodology
- PoR reports signed only by the platform itself
- Anonymous auditors or vague "internal security teams"
- No incident history at all (statistically implausible for any platform that has actually been operating)
- Past reports that have quietly been withdrawn or deleted

One data point from the wider sector is worth pausing on. KYC policy across spot exchanges varies a lot: only around 23% of exchanges require source-of-funds documentation, and roughly 29% require proof of address. The practical lesson is that a regulated-looking platform isn't necessarily a compliant one. You can't assume the controls are there. You have to check the specific policies in place.

The approach OKX takes, where Merkle-tree inclusion checks and public verification tooling are open to any user, is the kind of verifiable standard worth holding out for. Looking at [exchange security verification](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/navigating-the-shifting-sands-crypto-exchange-security-in-2026-2026-04-14) practices across a range of platforms shows just how rare that level of openness still is.

Keeping up with the [latest security incidents](https://cryptowatchdog.net/blog/latest-crypto-exchange-security-incidents-what-users-need-to-know-2026-04-17) matters just as much. A platform that passed your review six months ago might have had a material security event since. Ongoing monitoring is part of looking after your assets, not an extra.

**Pro Tip:** Always go looking for downloadable proof files or public verification tools. A score on a website is where your inquiry starts, not where it ends. If you can't independently confirm the data underneath it, treat the score as unverified and move on accordingly.

## Why methodology matters more than trust scores: A safety-first perspective

The most dangerous idea floating around crypto retail circles right now is that a single number, a trust score out of 100, can reliably tell you whether a platform is safe. We get the appeal. It's simple, it saves time, and it feels authoritative. But across dozens of platform reviews spanning exchanges, wallets, DeFi protocols and trading bots, opaque trust scores have been some of the most misleading signals we've come across.

The collapses that wiped out retail investors in recent cycles share a pattern. The platforms involved carried strong aggregate scores from various aggregators right up until they failed. Those scores rested on self-reported data, unverified claims and volume metrics that had nothing to do with solvency or custody. The score was real. The trustworthiness wasn't. That's the whole problem in one sentence.

As the Trusted Exchange Framework puts it plainly, when you're using any exchange review or safety ranking, prefer sources that expose their mechanics over ones that hand you an opaque trust score. This isn't a minor preference. It's the line that separates analysis from advertising.

We hold ourselves to the same standard. Our 8-point audit framework is published. Our scoring methodology is explained. When we run live deposit and withdrawal tests, we write down what we did, what happened, and what it means. The aim isn't a convincing number. It's evidence you can pick apart yourself.

What we'd encourage every reader to adopt is a "distrust-by-default" posture. That doesn't mean treating every platform as a scam. It means asking platforms to *earn* trust through verifiable evidence rather than claim it through marketing. A platform that publishes its proof of reserves, submits to independent auditing, names its leadership and documents its incident history is showing you something a score never can: a willingness to be held to account.

The discomfort of that posture is worth it. The alternative, extending trust on surface signals, is precisely how so many people have ended up locked out of accounts or watching balances vanish. Applying a rigorous process isn't being sceptical for the sake of it. It's being professional. In a market with this many structural risks, that professionalism is your main line of defence.

Treating platform trust as something to be verified rather than assumed is the single most useful mindset shift this guide can leave you with.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is proof-of-reserves (PoR) and why is it crucial for crypto platform reviews?

Proof-of-reserves is a verifiable audit showing that a platform holds enough assets to cover all user balances. It matters because, as OKX's public PoR programme demonstrates, user-level inclusion verification gives you concrete evidence of solvency that a self-reported claim simply can't.

### How do I check if a crypto exchange is trustworthy?

Look for transparent proof-of-reserves, named third-party auditors, a clear scoring methodology, regulatory registration, and inclusion tools you can verify yourself. A solid review method combines security auditing, proof verification and operational risk assessment rather than leaning on any single metric.

### Do all crypto exchanges require KYC verification?

No. KYC requirements vary widely across the sector. CoinDesk's Exchange Benchmark reports that only around 23% of spot exchanges require source-of-funds documentation and roughly 29% require proof of address, so you can't assume compliance rigour from appearance alone.

### What are common red flags when reviewing crypto platforms?

Missing or unverifiable proof-of-reserves, opaque trust scores with no published methodology, absent audit trails, and inconsistent regulatory disclosures are all key warning signs. As the Trusted Exchange Framework advises, always prefer review sources that expose their scoring mechanics over ones that produce scores without explanation.

### How often should I repeat safety reviews for platforms I use?

Run a structured review at least once a quarter, and again straight after any reported major incident or exploit affecting the platform or a close competitor. Security conditions in crypto change faster than in most traditional financial sectors, and a review from even three months ago may no longer reflect reality.
