## The short answer

A balance on a screen is not your money. It becomes your money only when you have successfully moved it off the platform and into a wallet or account you control.

That is why **a withdrawal test is the single most important check in any honest crypto review** — and why we run one on every platform we assess at CryptoWatchdog. Deposits are designed to be easy everywhere. Withdrawals are where scams, insolvent firms, and badly run businesses all fail in exactly the same way: the exit door is locked.

This guide explains why withdrawal testing matters, walks through real cases where platforms blocked withdrawals (some outright fraud, some collapsed "legitimate" firms), and gives you a step-by-step process to test any platform yourself before you risk meaningful capital.

## The illusion of profit: paper gains versus real money

In the early stages of any bull market, platforms promising astonishing returns appear like mushrooms after rain. They tend to share one feature: a slick dashboard showing your balance climbing fast. That is the point. The number is engineered to trigger excitement and convince you to deposit more.

But a number on a website is just a row in a database. It has no real-world value until it can be moved and spent elsewhere.

This is the digital equivalent of a casino that happily lets you "win" at the table but keeps no cash in the vault to pay out your chips. The game looks real. The payout does not exist.

The pattern shows up again and again:

- **Squid Game token (SQUID, 2021):** the contract let people buy in but blocked almost everyone from selling. The chart went vertical, then collapsed to near zero once the developers pulled liquidity.
- **Classic high-yield Ponzis:** users watched a balance grow daily on a lending dashboard, were encouraged to reinvest and recruit, and discovered the withdrawal button was the first thing disabled when the scheme ran out of new deposits.

Any "review" that does not verify the exit door is just reviewing the lobby's decor. This is why our methodology — see [how we test crypto trading bots and platforms](/blog/best-crypto-exchange-uk-2026) for the same evidence-led approach — always ends at a successful, real withdrawal.

## The "Hotel California" of crypto: how scams trap your funds

"You can check out any time you like, but you can never leave." That lyric is the perfect summary of a withdrawal scam. Getting money in is effortless. Getting it out is a nightmare.

Once you request a withdrawal, the excuses begin — and they are not random. They are scripted tactics designed to frustrate you into giving up or, worse, depositing more. Regulators and law enforcement describe these same patterns repeatedly. The US Federal Trade Commission warns that in investment scams, "the website looks legit, your balance is growing — but when you try to cash out, you can't," with platforms demanding fees or taxes first ([FTC: how to avoid a cryptocurrency scam](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/what-know-about-cryptocurrency-and-scams)).

Common traps include:

- **Endless verification.** You are asked for ID, then a utility bill, then a selfie holding your ID and a handwritten note. Each document is rejected for a trivial reason, resetting the clock.
- **Advance-fee fraud.** The platform says you must pay a "tax," "conversion fee," or "wallet activation fee" to release your withdrawal — and crucially, it cannot come from your existing balance. You must deposit *new* funds. That money is also lost. The US Secret Service notes that in these schemes victims are told they "must pay a percentage in tax" before withdrawing, which simply extracts more money ([US Secret Service: investment fraud and pig butchering](https://www.secretservice.gov/investigations/investmentfraud-pigbutchering)).
- **Sudden technical issues.** The platform announces "unscheduled maintenance" the moment you try to withdraw — while deposits keep working perfectly.
- **Surprise lock-ups.** You are suddenly told funds are subject to a 90-day staking period that was never in the terms you agreed to.

These tactics are rampant in less-regulated corners of the market. For live examples from our own desk, see our warnings on [the CryptoMine Pro mining scam](/warnings/cryptomine-pro-scam-warning) and [the YieldMax AI "guaranteed return" scam](/warnings/yieldmax-ai-scam-warning) — both follow the deposit-easy, withdraw-impossible script almost to the letter.

### Why the advance-fee "tax" is always a lie

If you remember one thing, remember this: **a legitimate platform never asks you to deposit new money to release money you already hold.** Withdrawal fees, where they exist, are deducted from your balance and disclosed up front. And no real tax authority is ever paid in crypto directly to the platform you are trying to leave. The moment you are asked to "pay tax to withdraw," the test is over and you have your answer.

## When "legitimate" platforms fail: liquidity crises and bank runs

Withdrawal risk is not limited to outright scams. The history of crypto is littered with once-credible firms that imploded and took customer funds with them. These businesses were not designed as scams from day one — but through mismanagement, fraud, or reckless risk-taking, they became insolvent and could not honour withdrawals.

The pattern is always the same: a rumour spreads, everyone rushes to the exit at once, and the platform freezes withdrawals because the assets simply are not there.

| Platform | When withdrawals halted | What we know |
|---|---|---|
| Mt. Gox | February 2014 | Halted withdrawals before collapsing; hundreds of thousands of BTC lost. Creditor repayments dragged on for over a decade. |
| Celsius Network | 12 June 2022 | Froze withdrawals citing "extreme market conditions," then filed Chapter 11 on 13 July 2022. |
| Voyager Digital | July 2022 | Suspended withdrawals and trading days before filing for bankruptcy. |
| FTX | 8 November 2022 | Halted withdrawals amid a liquidity crisis; filed for bankruptcy on 11 November 2022. |

**Celsius** froze customer withdrawals on 12 June 2022, trapping billions in user assets, and filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy a month later ([CoinDesk: the fall of Celsius Network, a timeline](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2022/07/15/the-fall-of-celsius-network-a-timeline-of-the-crypto-lenders-descent-into-insolvency)). It later emerged from bankruptcy and began distributing funds to creditors — but holders had no access for a long, painful period in between.

**FTX** halted withdrawals on 8 November 2022 after a wave of redemptions exposed a multi-billion-dollar shortfall, then filed for bankruptcy three days later. As detailed in the [Wikipedia summary of the FTX bankruptcy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bankruptcy_of_FTX), customer funds had been improperly funnelled to its affiliated trading firm, Alameda Research. A US court later approved a reorganisation plan to repay customers — but only after a long, court-supervised process, and only because assets were eventually recovered.

The lesson is uncomfortable but important: **even a withdrawal that works today is not a permanent guarantee.** That is why withdrawal testing pairs naturally with reducing how much you leave on any custodial platform in the first place — a theme we explore in [self-custody versus custodial wallets](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026).

## How big is the problem? What the data shows

Withdrawal-blocking fraud is not a fringe issue. According to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) 2024 Annual Report, victims reported **$9.3 billion** in cryptocurrency-related losses in 2024 — a 66% increase on the previous year — with cryptocurrency investment fraud alone accounting for **$5.8 billion** across more than 40,000 complaints ([FBI IC3 2024 Annual Report, PDF](https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2024_IC3Report.pdf)).

A defining feature of these "pig butchering" investment scams is precisely the trap this article describes: victims see a fake balance grow, are encouraged to deposit more, and then cannot withdraw — because the platform was never real. Blockchain analytics firm Chainalysis has tracked this category growing year on year as the operations become more sophisticated ([Chainalysis: 2024 pig butchering scam revenue](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2024-pig-butchering-scam-revenue-grows-yoy/)).

The over-60 age group reported both the most complaints and the highest losses in the IC3 data — a reminder that anyone can be targeted, and that a simple test withdrawal is a defence available to everyone.

## The litmus test: a practical guide to withdrawal testing

Here is the process we use, adapted so you can run it yourself on any new exchange, bot, or yield platform.

1. **Start small.** Pick an amount you would be genuinely comfortable losing — £20, £50, or £100. This is your test capital. Never make your first deposit a large one.
2. **Deposit from a known-good source.** Send your test capital from an account you already trust — for example, a major regulated exchange such as [Kraken](/reviews/kraken), or from a hardware wallet like a [Trezor](/reviews/trezor) or [Ledger Nano X](/reviews/ledger-nano-x).
3. **Let it settle, then wait.** You are not testing profitability — you are testing the plumbing. Give it a day or two.
4. **Withdraw the full amount.** Initiate a withdrawal for everything, back to the wallet or exchange it came from. Watch the process closely: are the fees stated up front? Is the interface honest?
5. **Analyse the result:**
   - **Success:** the funds arrive back, in a reasonable time (minutes for many on-chain transfers, up to roughly a day for some centralised exchanges) with no surprise charges. This is a genuine green flag.
   - **Failure:** you hit an error, a support agent gives an excuse, or you are asked to pay a new fee or "tax." Treat your test capital as lost, stop, and do not deposit another penny.

### A quick red-flag / green-flag checklist

| Green flags | Red flags |
|---|---|
| Withdrawal fees disclosed before you confirm | Fees or "taxes" revealed only at withdrawal |
| Funds arrive in a reasonable, predictable time | "Maintenance" appears exactly when you withdraw |
| Verification done once, sensibly, at sign-up | Endless, repeated ID requests that reset progress |
| Withdrawal address whitelisting and 2FA | Pressure to deposit more to "unlock" a withdrawal |
| Clear, checkable regulatory status | Requests to pay tax in crypto to the platform |

This costs very little and can save you from catastrophic loss. It is the most important step in any personal "is X safe?" assessment.

## Frequently asked questions

**How much should I use for a test withdrawal?**
Use the smallest amount that the platform allows above its minimum withdrawal threshold, and never more than you would be comfortable losing entirely. For most people that means somewhere in the £20–£100 range. The goal is to test the process, not to invest.

**Is a successful withdrawal a guarantee the platform is safe?**
No. It is strong evidence that the platform is functioning and willing to pay out *today*, which rules out the most common scams. But it does not protect you against future insolvency — as Celsius and FTX showed, a platform that paid out yesterday can freeze withdrawals tomorrow. Treat a passed test as necessary, not sufficient, and avoid leaving large balances parked on any custodial platform.

**The platform says I need to pay a tax or fee before I can withdraw. Is that normal?**
No. This is the single clearest sign of a scam. Legitimate platforms deduct any withdrawal fee from your existing balance and disclose it up front. No genuine service requires you to deposit *new* money to release money you already hold, and no real tax authority is paid in crypto directly to a trading platform. If you see this, stop immediately.

**My withdrawal is delayed. Does that automatically mean it is a scam?**
Not always. On-chain transactions can be slow during network congestion, and regulated exchanges sometimes apply security holds on new accounts or large or unusual withdrawals. The warning signs are *escalating* excuses, demands for more money, repeated identity checks that reset, or support that stops responding. A single delay with a clear, consistent explanation is different from a moving target.

**How do I test a platform that only supports staking or lock-up periods?**
Read the terms before depositing and confirm the exact unlock date and any conditions. Then deposit a small amount, wait for the shortest available lock-up to end, and withdraw. If the platform changes the rules, extends the lock-up, or introduces new conditions at withdrawal time, that is your red flag.

**Should I just keep my crypto in self-custody and avoid this entirely?**
Self-custody removes counterparty and withdrawal risk because no third party can freeze your funds — but it shifts responsibility for security entirely onto you, including seed-phrase backups. Many people use a hybrid approach: a regulated exchange for buying and selling, and a hardware wallet for long-term holdings. See [self-custody versus custodial wallets](/blog/self-custody-vs-custodial-wallets-2026) for the trade-offs.

**Where can I check if an exchange is properly regulated in the UK?**
Check the Financial Conduct Authority's register and its warning list of unauthorised firms directly on the FCA website — never rely on claims or logos shown on the platform's own pages, since cloned firms routinely fake them.

## The bottom line

Everything about a crypto platform — the app, the fees, the testimonials, the returns — is downstream of one question: can you get your money out? Scams and collapsed firms fail in the same place, at the exit door, which is why a real withdrawal is the only test that cannot be faked.

So before you trust any platform with serious money, run the test yourself: start small, deposit from a source you control, and withdraw the full amount back. If it works cleanly, you have meaningful evidence. If it does not, you have just learned the most important thing about that platform for the price of a small, deliberate stake.
