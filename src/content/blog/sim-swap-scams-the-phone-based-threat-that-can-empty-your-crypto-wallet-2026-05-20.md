# SIM Swap Scams: The Phone-Based Threat That Can Empty Your Crypto Wallet

Picture this. Your phone drops to "No Service" mid-morning. Annoying, but you shrug it off as the network having a bad day. A couple of hours later an email pings on your laptop: a withdrawal confirmation for money you never moved. You call your mobile provider, finally get through, and learn your number now lives in someone else's phone. By then the exchange account is empty.

That is a SIM swap, and it is one of the nastier crypto threats because it walks straight past a strong password. The attacker never touches your phone, your computer, or your wallet. They go after the phone *number* tied to your accounts, and once they have it, they catch the codes that are supposed to be protecting your money.

Here is the part people find hard to accept: you can do almost everything right and still get caught, because the weak link is sitting in a call centre you have never spoken to. So this guide does two things. It shows you exactly how the attack runs, and it walks through the defences that actually move the needle. We will tell you straight what is proven, what is murky, and what no single gadget can promise.

## Key takeaways

- **Your phone number is a soft spot, not a strongbox.** The instant your number is ported to an attacker's SIM, those SMS codes land on their screen instead of yours.
- **SIM swap losses run into the tens of millions of dollars a year** in FBI-reported figures alone, and the real total is almost certainly higher because most victims never file a report.
- **App- or hardware-key two-factor authentication (2FA) beats SMS by a mile.** Where a service offers it, reach for a passkey, an authenticator app, or a FIDO2 security key.
- **Self-custody takes the exchange out of the firing line.** A [hardware wallet](/blog/best-hardware-wallet-2026-ledger-vs-trezor) keeps your private keys offline, so a stolen phone number on its own cannot move your coins.
- **Phone the carrier and lock the account yourself** with a PIN or a port-freeze, and start treating your recovery email like the crown jewels, because to an attacker it is exactly that.

## What is a SIM swap attack?

A SIM swap, sometimes called SIM jacking or a port-out scam, is account-takeover fraud aimed at your phone number. The thing to understand is who gets fooled. It is not you, and it is not your handset. It is your *mobile carrier*. The attacker talks their way into your account by pretending to be you.

The attack usually runs in three stages.

1. **Reconnaissance.** The criminal collects the basics about you: full name, date of birth, address, who your carrier is, your number. This stuff is depressingly easy to gather, scraped from old data breaches, lifted off public social media, or coaxed out of you with an earlier phishing message.
2. **Social engineering.** Now wearing your identity, the attacker rings the carrier's support line or strolls into a store. The phone was lost, they say, or smashed, and they would like the number moved to a new SIM they happen to be holding. When asked security questions, they read back the answers they already collected.
3. **The swap.** If staff buy the story, your number is "ported" to the attacker's SIM. Your phone goes dark in that moment, and every call and text after it, your 2FA codes and password-reset links included, rings on the criminal's device.

A 2024 case shows how little technical skill this takes. When the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's account on X was hijacked that January, the man behind it did not breach a server. He pulled together a victim's personal details, made a fake ID, walked into a phone store, and walked out with a SIM tied to the victim's number. He was later [sentenced to 14 months in prison](https://www.justice.gov/usao-dc/pr/alabama-man-sentenced-hack-sec-x-account-spiked-value-bitcoin) and admitted he was paid around $50,000 for the swap. The entry fee for this crime is a believable story and a printed card. That is the uncomfortable truth here, and it is exactly why it keeps happening.

## How big is the problem? What the data shows

It is tempting to file SIM swapping under "rare, happens to other people." The official numbers say otherwise, and remember, those numbers only count the people who actually came forward.

The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) has tracked it for years, and the figures move around a fair bit:

| Period | SIM swap complaints (US) | Reported losses (US) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jan 2018 – Dec 2020 | 320 | ~$12 million |
| 2021 | 1,611 | $68 million+ |
| 2022 | 2,026 | $72.6 million |
| 2023 | 1,075 | $48.8 million |
| 2024 | 982 | $26.0 million |

Sources: the FBI's [2022 public service announcement on SIM swapping](https://www.ic3.gov/PSA/2022/PSA220208) and later IC3 annual reporting.

A few honest caveats, because the headline numbers are easy to misread:

- **These are reported losses only.** Plenty of victims never file, often out of embarrassment or because they assume nothing can be done. The true totals sit higher than the table.
- **Fewer complaints is not the same as a fading threat.** A single case can be vast, and how willing people are to report shifts year to year.
- **Crypto is heavily over-represented in the biggest losses,** because once crypto moves, it tends to stay moved.

That last point is the whole game. Court records spell out the stakes: U.S. prosecutors have gone after social-engineering crews responsible for hundreds of millions in crypto theft, including a 2025 case where a defendant pleaded guilty to a scheme that drained an estimated [$243 million from a single victim](https://www.theblock.co/post/370072/us-justice-dept-files-civil-forfeiture-complaint-for-5-million-in-bitcoin-stolen-via-sim-swap-attacks) using a blend of SIM swaps and fake support calls. We cannot tell you whether you will be a target. We can tell you the downside is brutal, and it is worth a few hours of your time to close the door.

## Why crypto is the perfect prize

So why bother with all this effort? Because your phone number has quietly turned into the master key to your whole digital life, and crypto is the cleanest thing in the house to walk off with.

A dodgy bank transfer can often be flagged, frozen, or clawed back. A confirmed on-chain transaction usually cannot. Once the funds leave an exchange and bounce through a couple of wallets, there is no fraud department to ring and no chargeback button to press. That finality is one of crypto's real strengths. It is also why a theft lands so hard.

A typical attack chain runs like this:

1. The attacker goes for your **email** first, because email is the recovery hub for everything else. They hit "Forgot password" and ask for the code by text, which now arrives on their phone, not yours.
2. With the inbox open, they search for words like "Kraken," "Coinbase," "withdrawal," or "wallet" to map out every platform you touch.
3. They hit each exchange, fire off a password reset, and combine your email with the intercepted SMS codes to walk through any weak 2FA.
4. They set their own withdrawal address and send your balance out the door.

The lesson is an uncomfortable one: your security is only as strong as its weakest link, and that link might be a carrier rep having a busy afternoon. A well-run, careful exchange can do its job properly and still get bypassed because the attacker came in through your hijacked number. This is a different beast from outright fraud like the operations we pull apart in our [CryptoMine Pro scam warning](/warnings/cryptomine-pro-scam-warning) and [YieldMax AI scam warning](/warnings/yieldmax-ai-scam-warning). There, the platform itself is the scam. With a SIM swap, the platform may be perfectly legitimate; the threat is a recovery path you never realised you had left open.

## Your biggest weak point: SMS two-factor authentication

For years the advice was lovely and simple: turn on 2FA everywhere. Still good advice. But the *kind* of 2FA you choose matters enormously, and in 2026 leaning on SMS text codes is one of the weakest choices on the menu.

The Short Message Service was dreamt up in the 1980s for sending short messages. Nobody designed it to guard your savings. It is unencrypted as it travels, and, as we have just seen, it is welded to a phone number that can be stolen while the handset never leaves your pocket.

Here is how the common 2FA methods stack up against a SIM swap, specifically.

| 2FA method | Resists SIM swap? | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SMS / voice call code | No | Codes follow the number straight to the attacker. |
| Authenticator app (TOTP) | Yes | Codes are generated on your device, never sent over the network. |
| Push approval app | Mostly | Strong, but stay alert for "approval fatigue" prompt-spamming. |
| Hardware security key (FIDO2/U2F) | Yes | The strongest widely available option, and phishing-resistant. |
| Passkey | Yes | Device-bound, resists both phishing and SMS interception. |

The takeaway is plain. Wherever a service supports it, move off SMS and onto an authenticator app, a passkey, or a physical security key. When you are sizing up an exchange, check what it accepts for login and, just as importantly, for *withdrawals*, which is where the money actually leaves. A platform that still treats SMS as a primary recovery method is leaving a door propped open, however polished everything else looks. ## The strongest defence: self-custody with a hardware wallet

Real security in crypto comes down to trusting as few parties as you can get away with, and yes, that includes your mobile carrier. For holdings that genuinely matter to you, the most solid move is to take them off exchanges and into self-custody with a hardware wallet.

A device like a [Ledger Nano X](/reviews/ledger-nano-x) or a [Trezor](/reviews/trezor) keeps your private keys on an offline device that never plugs its secrets into an internet-connected computer or phone. So even if an attacker SIM swaps you, takes over your email, and roots around on your laptop, they still cannot move your funds without physically holding the device and knowing its PIN. That is a tall order for someone working a phone scam from another country.

## Lock down the exchange side too

If you keep some funds on an exchange for convenience or for trading, switch on every control it hands you:

- **Hardware-key login (FIDO2/U2F)** where it is supported, in place of SMS.
- **Withdrawal address whitelisting,** so funds can only ever go to addresses you approved in advance.
- **Time delays** on changes to security settings or on new withdrawal addresses, which force an attacker to sit and wait, and waiting is exactly what they cannot do quietly.
- **Anti-phishing codes,** a personal phrase the exchange drops into its genuine emails so you can spot a fake at a glance.
- **Login and withdrawal alerts** sent to a channel the attacker does not control.

None of this makes an account unbreakable. What it does is turn a fast, silent theft into a slow, noisy one, and noise is what gives you the minutes you need to react.

## What carriers and regulators are doing

You are not completely on your own here. In the United States, the FCC adopted rules in November 2023 requiring wireless providers to use secure customer authentication before moving a number to a new SIM or carrier, and to tell customers straight away when a SIM change or port-out is requested. You can read the FCC's own summary of the [rules to protect consumers' cell phone accounts](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-adopts-rules-protect-consumers-cell-phone-accounts-0).

It is a genuine improvement, and we will take it. But it is not a guarantee. Social engineering still gets past staff, fake IDs still get waved at store counters, and the rules differ from one country to the next. Treat regulation as a backstop behind your own precautions, not a replacement for them.

## Action checklist: what to do right now

Do not wait for "No Service" to teach you this. A spare half-hour today is worth far more than a frantic afternoon later.

- **Add a carrier PIN or port-freeze.** Ring your provider and set up a verbal password, account PIN, or "port-out lock" that has to be given before any SIM change goes through.
- **Strip SMS out as a 2FA method** on every account that lets you, and swap in an authenticator app, passkey, or hardware key.
- **Spin up a dedicated crypto email.** Use a fresh address for exchange and wallet accounts only, protect it with a hardware key or passkey, and keep it off the public internet.
- **Audit your accounts.** Write down every exchange, wallet, and financial service tied to your phone number, then move each one onto stronger 2FA.
- **Shift long-term holdings into self-custody** on a hardware wallet, leaving only active funds on exchanges.
- **Turn on withdrawal whitelisting and alerts** for every exchange you use.
- **Shrink your data footprint.** Be sparing about what you share publicly on your phone number, your finances, and your crypto.
- **Have a response plan.** Know your carrier's fraud line and how to freeze your exchange accounts, so you can move within minutes if the phone goes dark.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can a SIM swap happen even if I still have my physical phone?**

Yes, and this is the bit that trips people up. The criminal does not want your handset. They talk your carrier into moving your *number* onto a different SIM, and from that moment your phone loses service while their device starts catching your calls and texts. You might not notice until you go to use it and find nothing works.

**Is an authenticator app really safer than SMS?**

For resisting SIM swaps, yes. Authenticator apps generate codes locally on your device from a shared secret, so the code never crosses the mobile network and cannot be scooped up by hijacking your number. A hardware security key or passkey is stronger again, because it also stands up to phishing. No method is flawless, but app- and key-based 2FA close the exact gap SMS leaves wide open.

**Will a hardware wallet completely protect me from a SIM swap?**

It protects the assets stored on it, because the private keys stay offline and nothing can be signed without the device and its PIN. That shuts down remote theft of self-custodied funds. What it does not do is protect funds you leave on an exchange, and it does not stop the social-engineering and email-takeover parts of the attack. Use it alongside strong 2FA and carrier protections, not as your only line of defence.

**What should I do the moment I suspect a SIM swap?**

Move quickly, and try not to panic. Contact your mobile carrier from another phone to report the fraud and get your number back. Then, from a device you trust, change passwords and lock or freeze your exchange and email accounts, pausing withdrawals if you can. Report it to your local cybercrime or fraud authority. In the U.S., you can file a complaint with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. If this has already happened to you, none of it was a sign you were careless; these crews are practised, and they fool trained call-centre staff for a living.

**Are exchanges or carriers liable if I lose crypto to a SIM swap?**

This one is genuinely uncertain, and it turns on the facts, the jurisdiction, and the contracts in play. Some victims have taken carriers to court over sloppy identity checks, with mixed results. Crypto's irreversibility makes recovery hard even when the blame is obvious. Because compensation is never a given, prevention is the far more reliable bet than hoping to be made whole afterwards.

**Does porting my number to an eSIM make me safer?**

Not on its own. The attack goes after your carrier's account controls, not the physical card, so an eSIM does not block it by itself. What actually helps is the account-level stuff: a strong carrier PIN, a port-out lock, and breach notifications. Pair those with non-SMS 2FA and you get the real benefit.

## Where this leaves you

SIM swapping works because it attacks the recovery paths around your accounts rather than the accounts themselves. The fix is to take your phone number out of the centre of your security: lock down the carrier account, replace SMS codes with app- or key-based 2FA, guard your recovery email, and move the holdings you care about into self-custody on a hardware wallet.

None of this is glamorous, and not one step is a guarantee on its own. Stack them together, though, and you close the easy openings these attacks rely on. That is the quiet difference between the people who keep their crypto and the people who end up as a line in next year's IC3 report.
