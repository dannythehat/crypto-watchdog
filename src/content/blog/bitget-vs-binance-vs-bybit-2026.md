## Copy trading — Bitget's standout

If there is one feature where Bitget clearly leads this trio, it is **copy trading**. Bitget was one of the first major exchanges to push social trading hard, and independent reviewers consistently describe it as one of the largest copy-trading platforms by participant numbers.

How it works in plain English:

- You browse a leaderboard of **elite traders**, filtered by metrics like win rate, drawdown and assets traded.
- You allocate funds to mirror one or more of them; your account **automatically copies their trades** in proportion to what you set.
- The trader earns a **profit share, typically around 8–10%** of the gains you make — and crucially, that is usually charged **on top of normal trading fees only when you profit**, not as an extra blanket fee.

Bybit and Binance both offer copy or social-trading features too, but the depth of Bitget's trader pool and discovery tools is the differentiator that most comparisons highlight.

A safety caveat we always add: copy trading is **not** passive, guaranteed income. You are inheriting another person's risk appetite and leverage. Past performance on a leaderboard does not predict future results, traders can blow up, and high-leverage strategies can lose money fast. Start small, read our guide to [copy trading](/blog/what-is-copy-crypto-trading-your-2026-guide) if you are new, and never allocate money you cannot afford to lose. To learn more about how copy trading works on the platform, see our [Bitget review](/reviews/bitget).

For traders whose centre of gravity is **perpetual futures**, Bybit is the specialist. It has built its reputation on a **derivatives-first** platform: a low-latency matching engine, deep perpetuals liquidity, and **high leverage** (some products reach well beyond the 100x mark, with certain contracts going higher still).

When people say "the big exchange", they usually mean Binance — and the data backs the reputation. Binance generally posts the **deepest order books** and the **widest selection** of coins and products of any centralised venue.

Why that matters for you:

## Security and protection funds

None of these are FCA-protected, and **none carry government deposit insurance** like a UK bank's FSCS. What they offer instead are **self-funded protection pools** — pots of the exchange's own money earmarked to reimburse users after certain security incidents. That is better than nothing, but it is discretionary and not a legal guarantee.

What the public record shows:

- **Binance's SAFU** is the best-known model and is **reported to exceed $1 billion**, built by routing a share of fee revenue into the fund.
- **Bitget's Protection Fund** has published regular valuation reports; in **early 2026 it was reported around the $450 million mark**, with Bitget committing to keep it above its $300m launch floor.
- **Bybit** maintains an insurance fund, though its size is not as consistently disclosed in public comparisons.

Two honest points. First, a protection fund is only as good as the exchange's willingness and ability to deploy it — it is not a contractual guarantee. Second, Bybit suffered a very large security incident in early 2025 that it weathered, a reminder that even big, well-capitalised exchanges are targets. The single best protection you control is **not leaving more on any exchange than you need to trade**.
