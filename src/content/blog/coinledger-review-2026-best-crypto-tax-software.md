## A deeper look at how tax authorities are tracking crypto in 2026

To understand why CoinLedger has become essential rather than optional, you have to understand what changed on the enforcement side.

In 2025, the US Treasury finalised the broker reporting rules under section 6045. Beginning with the 2025 tax year, every centralised exchange operating in the US must issue Form 1099-DA to both the customer and the IRS. That form lists every taxable disposal during the year. In other words, the IRS already has the data before you file.

In the UK, HMRC has run nudge-letter campaigns aimed at identified Coinbase, Binance and Kraken users every year since 2021. The letters specifically reference data shared by the exchange under the international information exchange treaty. By 2027, the OECD Crypto Asset Reporting Framework expands this globally across more than 50 participating countries.

Put plainly: tax authorities know what you traded, when, and roughly how much. If your filed return doesn't match the data they already hold, you become an audit target. The cost of getting it wrong now far outweighs the cost of getting it right the first time.

That's the real value of CoinLedger. Not just convenience — peace of mind that the numbers you file are the numbers the taxman is already looking at.

## Edge cases the software handles well

We threw a deliberately ugly portfolio at CoinLedger to see where it would break. It held up in places we expected it to stumble.

**Wrapped tokens.** A swap from ETH to WETH is not a taxable event in most jurisdictions. CoinLedger correctly treats it as a same-asset wrap, not a sale.

**Failed transactions.** Gas spent on a failed swap is still a deductible cost in some jurisdictions. CoinLedger captures it as a fee event.

**Spam tokens.** That random airdrop of garbage tokens worth $0 should not appear as income. CoinLedger has a spam filter that catches the most common drainer airdrops automatically.
