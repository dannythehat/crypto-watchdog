## What is "tokenization", really? (the lemonade-stand version)

Imagine your friend owns a lemonade stand. It makes $100 a week. You want to buy a slice of it but you don't have much money.

So your friend writes "1/100 of the lemonade stand" on a hundred paper tickets. You buy one ticket for $5. Now you legally own one percent of the stand and one percent of the profits.

That's tokenization. The stand is the real asset. The ticket is the token. A blockchain is just a fancy notebook that tracks who holds which ticket — and pays out the right share of profits, automatically.

Now swap "lemonade stand" for "Manhattan apartment building", "US Treasury bill", or "gold bar in a London vault". Same idea. Bigger numbers. We cover the full landscape on our [RWA Tokenization hub](/rwa-tokenization).

## Why bother? Why not just… buy the thing?

Fair question. Here's why people do it.

**Tiny minimums.** A US Treasury bill normally needs $1,000+ to buy. A tokenized one can be bought for $1. A whole rental property can cost $400,000. A token of one might cost $50.

**24/7 markets.** Stocks, bonds and real estate trade during business hours. Tokens trade every second of every day, anywhere in the world, including weekends.

**Instant settlement.** When you buy a stock today, you legally own it two business days later. With a token, you own it before your kettle finishes boiling.

**No middlemen.** No broker, no transfer agent, no paper deed sitting in a filing cabinet in New Jersey. The blockchain replaces most of the plumbing.

That's the upside. The downside is that "instant" and "permissionless" also make it easier for con artists. We'll get to that.

## The deed is not the house (the most important sentence in this article)

Read this twice. The token is not the asset. The token is a digital deed that points at the asset.

If someone sells you a token that says "this represents a Lamborghini" but there's no actual Lamborghini, no garage, no registration, no legal contract — congratulations, you bought a sticker.

A real RWA token has four things behind it.

### 1. The actual asset

A bar of gold in a vault. A bond in a custody account. A building with a deed. The thing must really exist and be inventoried.

### 2. A custodian

A regulated company whose only job is to hold the asset and not lose it. Banks, vaults, title companies. Boring on purpose.

### 3. A legal wrapper

Usually a special-purpose vehicle (SPV) or trust. The SPV legally owns the asset. Token holders legally own the SPV. That's the chain of custody that makes the token enforceable in court.

### 4. An issuer

The company that mints the tokens, runs the KYC, handles redemptions, and publishes the audits.

If any of those four are missing, you don't own a tokenized asset. You own a screenshot. The full checklist lives on our [RWA hub's "what backs a token" section](/rwa-tokenization).

## A quick tour of what's actually being tokenized

Tokenization isn't one thing — it's six. Each works differently. We track each category in detail on the [RWA Tokenization hub](/rwa-tokenization).

### Tokenized US Treasuries

Short-dated US government bills, wrapped into a token. Pays out the same yield as the bill itself, minus a fee. The biggest, safest, most boring slice of the market.

This is what BlackRock's BUIDL fund does. It's also what Ondo and Franklin Templeton do. Together they manage billions.

### Tokenized real estate

Buildings — usually rental properties — split into thousands of tokens. You buy some, you get a slice of the rent paid to your wallet in stablecoins every month.

Think of it as Airbnb-meets-stock-market for landlords.

### Tokenized gold

A vault holds a bar of physical gold. The vault issues a token. You buy the token, you own that gold (or its equivalent value). You can usually redeem the token for the actual bar if you want.

The two big ones are PAXG (from Paxos) and XAUT (from Tether).

### Tokenized private credit

Loans to real businesses — invoice financing, working capital, trade finance — packaged on-chain. Higher yields than treasuries, because the loans are riskier and less liquid.

### Tokenized art and collectibles

Fine art, watches, fancy wine. The piece sits in a climate-controlled storage unit. You buy fractional tokens. If the piece sells later for more, your tokens are worth more.

Riskier and more speculative. The art market is opinion-driven and slow.

### Tokenized stocks and funds

Shares of real public companies, or slices of private investment funds, represented as tokens. Mostly available to accredited or non-US investors today, because regulators are still catching up.

## How big is this, really?

Bigger than the headlines suggest, smaller than the hype implies.

As of late 2025, the on-chain RWA market — excluding stablecoins — sits around **$18 billion**, up from about $5 billion in 2022. That's roughly 380% growth in three years.

BlackRock's tokenized fund alone holds over $2 billion. Tokenized US Treasuries as a category sit around $7.5 billion. Private credit is the biggest slice at roughly $14 billion of represented value.

Stablecoins, which are technically also tokenized assets (tokenized US dollars), are much larger — over $200 billion. Most reports keep them in their own bucket because they're already mainstream.

## "OK, what could go wrong?" (the honest list)

Plenty. Tokenization solves some problems and creates others. Here are the realistic risks.

### The asset risk doesn't go away

If you buy a tokenized bond and the issuer defaults, you lose money. The blockchain doesn't fix bad credit. It just records the loss faster.

### The platform risk is new

You now also rely on the issuer not getting hacked, not going bankrupt, not running off with the keys, and not making a smart-contract mistake that locks everyone out.

### The redemption risk is real

In a calm market, you can swap your token back for the underlying. In a panic, that line gets long. Some platforms have queue limits. Read the small print.

### The legal risk is regional

Most RWA tokens are securities. That means they're regulated. That means the platform may not be allowed to sell to people in your country, and you may not be allowed to buy. Always check.

### The "trust me" risk

If a platform won't tell you who custodies the asset, where the SPV is registered, or how reserves are audited — they're asking you to trust a logo. Don't. This is exactly the pattern we break down in our [scam guides library](/scam-guides).

## How to spot a fake "tokenized" project

Most RWA scams follow the same pattern. Once you see it, you'll see it everywhere.

**They lead with the yield, not the asset.** Real RWA platforms tell you what the asset is. Scams tell you what the APY is.

**The yield doesn't match the asset.** Tokenized US Treasuries can't pay 30% APY. The Treasury bill itself pays 4–5%. If the math doesn't work, the asset isn't real.

**The custodian is "us".** A legit RWA platform names a separate, regulated custodian — not itself.

**No one's name is on it.** Anonymous teams cannot legally tokenize real-world assets. The paperwork requires a real human signature.

**There's no audit, just "proof of reserves" screenshots.** A screenshot is not an audit. A real audit is performed by a named third-party firm and published.

**You can't redeem.** "You can sell your tokens to other users" is not redemption. Redemption means the issuer hands you back the underlying or its dollar value.

## So is RWA tokenization worth it?

For the right person and the right product, yes — and we think it's one of the most important shifts in crypto right now.

A tokenized US Treasury from a regulated issuer is, genuinely, one of the safest yield products you can hold on-chain. A fractional rental property in a state with strong landlord-tenant law can be a perfectly legitimate way to access an asset class that was off-limits ten years ago.

But "tokenized" is a label, not a guarantee. The technology copies the asset's claim onto a blockchain. It does not magically make a bad asset good, or a fake asset real.

Read the paperwork. Check the custodian. Verify the audit. And — as always — never deposit more than you can afford to lose.
