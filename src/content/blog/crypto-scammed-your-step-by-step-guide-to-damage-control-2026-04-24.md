# Crypto Scammed? Your Step-by-Step Guide to Damage Control

The sickening drop in your stomach when you realise the funds are gone. The mix of panic, anger, and embarrassment is a feeling thousands in crypto know all too well. If you're reading this because you've just been scammed, take a deep breath; acting rashly now can make things worse.

## TL;DR

*   **Secure What's Left:** Immediately move any remaining funds from the compromised wallet or exchange to a brand new, secure wallet that you control. Do not reuse anything associated with the scam.
*   **Gather All Evidence:** Compile a complete dossier of the scam. This includes transaction IDs, scammer's wallet addresses, screenshots of conversations, and URLs of fake websites.
*   **Report, Report, Report:** File a report with Action Fraud (in the UK) or your local law enforcement. Also, report the scam addresses to the exchanges the funds were sent to, such as Binance or KuCoin.
*   **Beware Recovery Scams:** Anyone contacting you promising to recover your stolen crypto for an upfront fee is a scammer. This is a common and cruel follow-up attack.
*   **Learn and Fortify:** Use this painful lesson to drastically improve your security. Invest in a hardware wallet, learn to identify red flags, and stick to vetted platforms.

## First, Breathe. Then, Triage.

Your first instinct might be to frantically try and "reverse" the transaction or argue with the scammer. Let's be clear: crypto transactions are, by design, irreversible. The funds are gone from your immediate control. Your mission now is not reversal, but damage limitation.

The first and most critical step is to perform digital triage. Assume the wallet, computer, or phone you used is compromised.

1.  **Go Offline:** If your software wallet (like MetaMask or Phantom) was compromised, disconnect your computer from the internet immediately to prevent further unauthorised access.
2.  **Create a Safe Haven:** On a *different, trusted device* if possible, create a brand new, clean crypto wallet. Do not import your old, compromised seed phrase. This is a fresh start. A hardware wallet is the gold standard here; if you don't have one, now is the time to order one from the official manufacturer, like a [Ledger Nano X](/reviews/ledger-nano-x) or Trezor.
3.  **Evacuate Remaining Assets:** If you have any other tokens or NFTs in the compromised wallet that the scammer hasn't yet taken, immediately transfer them to your new, clean wallet address. Be quick. Scammers often use sweeper bots that automatically drain any new funds deposited into a compromised wallet. Do not transfer any funds *into* the compromised wallet.

This is especially critical if you were tricked into signing a malicious smart contract, a common tactic in [fake airdrop wallet drainer attacks](/warnings/fake-airdrop-wallet-drainer). Such a contract might give the scammer permission to drain specific tokens from your wallet at will.

Revoking these permissions using a tool like Revoke.cash should be done, but evacuating assets to a new wallet is the safest bet.

## Become a Digital Detective: Gather Evidence

Emotion is high, but now you need to be methodical. You are building a case file. The chances of law enforcement recovering your funds are slim, but not zero. More importantly, this evidence can help exchanges freeze accounts and prevent the scammers from cashing out easily.

Create a document and collect the following:

*   **Transaction Hashes (TXIDs):** Every crypto transaction has a unique ID. Go to a blockchain explorer (like Etherscan for Ethereum, or Solscan for Solana) and find the transaction(s) where you sent funds to the scammer. Copy the full TXID.
*   **Scammer's Addresses:** Note down the exact wallet addresses you sent crypto to.
*   **Your Addresses:** Note down the wallet addresses you sent crypto *from*.
*   **Communication Records:** Screenshot everything. Telegram messages, Discord DMs, emails, tweets, website forms. Do not rely on the platform; scammers often delete their accounts and messages. Save the images and back them up.
*   **Website URLs:** If the scam involved a fake website, save the full URL.
*   **A Narrative:** Write a clear, chronological account of what happened. When were you first contacted? What were you promised? What steps did you take? This will be invaluable when you file official reports.

## The Reporting Trail: Who to Tell and Why

Shouting into the void on Twitter feels good, but it doesn't create an official paper trail. Reporting needs to be strategic.

1.  **Law Enforcement:** In the UK, you must report the crime to **Action Fraud**. They are the national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime. Be prepared to provide all the evidence you gathered in the previous step. The reality is that local police are ill-equipped to handle most crypto cases, but an Action Fraud report is the necessary first step for any potential future insurance claim or legal action.
2.  **Exchanges:** This is often the most effective step. Scammers eventually need to send their stolen crypto to a centralised exchange like [Binance](/reviews/binance) or Coinbase to convert it to cash. These exchanges have compliance teams. File a support ticket with any exchange you can identify the funds moving to. Provide them with the transaction hashes and scammer addresses. If you can prove the funds landed in one of their accounts, they may be able to freeze the account, cutting off the scammer's exit route.
3.  **Community Warnings:** Warn others. If you were scammed in a specific project's Telegram or Discord group, post a clear, factual warning. Don't be emotional or abusive. Simply state the facts, share the scammer's username (if possible), and describe the method they used. You could prevent someone else from falling into the same trap.

## The Vultures Are Circling: Beware Recovery Scams

This is a cruel but predictable part of the cycle. Once you post online about being scammed, you will be inundated with DMs and messages from people claiming they can help. They will say they are "ethical hackers," "blockchain investigators," or "asset recovery specialists."

**Let's be absolutely clear: 99.9% of these are recovery scams.**

They will ask for an upfront fee to "initiate the trace" or "bribe an exchange employee." They will show you fake screenshots of your funds sitting in a wallet, just waiting to be unlocked once you pay their "success fee." This is a lie. They are preying on your desperation.

We've seen countless operators like this, and our [damning review of CryptoRecoveryPro](/reviews/crypto-recovery-pro) shows exactly how they operate.

Never, ever pay someone an upfront fee to recover stolen crypto. Legitimate blockchain analytics firms work with law enforcement and corporations, not individuals via Telegram DMs. The moment you are contacted by one of these "helpers", you are being targeted for a second scam.

Ignore them, block them, and report them. Our team has issued specific alerts on the rise of these schemes, which you can read in our guide on [recovery scams targeting crypto victims](/warnings/recovery-scam-targeting-victims).

## Safer Alternatives: Rebuilding with Better Bricks

The crypto space is not a complete wild west. The key is to stay on the well-lit paths and avoid the dark alleys where scams like the infamous [BitConnect](/reviews/bitconnect) Ponzi scheme fester. When you're ready to re-engage with crypto, do so with a completely new security mindset.

*   **Self-Custody:** Instead of leaving funds on an exchange, use a hardware wallet like a Ledger or Trezor. This keeps your private keys offline, making them immune to online hacks.
*   **Vetted DeFi:** Not all DeFi is a scam. Stick to protocols that are battle-tested, audited, and have been operating for years. Platforms like [Uniswap](/reviews/uniswap) and Aave on Ethereum, or established Layer 2 networks like [Arbitrum](/reviews/arbitrum), are fundamentally more trustworthy than a brand-new farm promising 1,000,000% APY. We saw what happened with unsustainable yields in the collapse of the [Anchor Protocol](/reviews/anchor-protocol), a lesson that cost investors billions.
*   **Reputable Exchanges:** If you must use a centralised entity for trading or on-ramping, stick to the largest and most regulated players. An exchange with a physical headquarters and a clear regulatory footprint is always preferable to an anonymous offshore entity.

## Action Checklist: Fortifying Your Defences

Use this painful experience as the catalyst for a complete security overhaul. Tick every item below before you touch crypto again.

*   **Buy a hardware wallet** from the official manufacturer's website only — never Amazon or eBay.
*   **Generate a brand-new seed phrase** and write it on paper or steel. Never store it digitally, never photograph it, never type it into a website.
*   **Enable a strong, unique password** and app-based 2FA (not SMS) on every exchange and email account you use.
*   **Bookmark the official URLs** of every platform you use. Never reach a dApp via a Google ad, a DM, or a search result.
*   **Use a fresh "burner" wallet** with minimal funds for any new dApp, mint or airdrop interaction.
*   **Revoke old token approvals** at least once a quarter using a trusted approval-checker.
*   **Read the audit before you deposit.** Stick to platforms we've cleared in our [vetted reviews](/reviews) and avoid anything not covered by our [8-point methodology](/methodology).

Being scammed is devastating — but it does not have to be the end of your crypto journey. Treat the loss as tuition. The traders and investors who survive long-term in this space are the ones who learn from a mistake once and never repeat it.
