Few crypto brands are as recognisable as **Crypto.com**. The arena in Los Angeles carries its name, its logo has been on Formula 1 cars and World Cup boards, and millions of people first heard "fortune favours the brave" from a Matt Damon advert. For a lot of seasoned crypto users, that marketing is exactly the problem — it *feels* like hype, and hype makes people nervous.

So we did what we always do: ignored the adverts and looked at the plumbing. This is our **master review of the whole Crypto.com ecosystem** — the exchange, the beginner app, the Visa card, the NFT marketplace, the self-custody wallet and the Earn product. Where a section deserves its own deep-dive, we link to a focused review further down.

> **The short version:** Crypto.com is a real, licensed business with proof-of-reserves attestations and a clean record of paying withdrawals — including full reimbursement after its 2022 hack. It is **custodial**, the card cashback is a shadow of its 2021 self, and the app's instant-buy is expensive. Use it well and it's a solid mainstream platform. Use it lazily and it'll quietly cost you.

## Is Crypto.com safe?

Safety, for us, isn't a feeling — it's a checklist: who runs it, where it's regulated, what happens to your money if something breaks, and whether you can actually get your funds out.

On every count, Crypto.com clears the bar that separates a real operator from a scam. It's run by a **named, public team**, it operates through **registered legal entities**, it holds a **MiCA licence in the EU** plus registrations and approvals across multiple jurisdictions, and it publishes **third-party proof-of-reserves attestations** you can check yourself on its transparency page. Most customer assets sit in **cold storage**, with MPC custody, 2FA, anti-phishing codes and a withdrawal whitelist on top.

None of that makes it risk-free — it's still custodial, which we'll come back to — but it puts Crypto.com firmly in the "legitimate" column.

## The 2022 hack — and why the response matters more

In **January 2022**, attackers bypassed two-factor authentication on roughly **483 accounts** and made off with around **$34 million** in crypto. That's a serious failure, and we don't wave it away.

What happened next is the part that actually tells you something. Crypto.com **reimbursed every affected user in full**, publicly acknowledged the breach, and shipped concrete fixes: a mandatory **24-hour hold** when you add a new withdrawal address, an account-protection program for unauthorised withdrawals, and additional authentication layers. 

Every platform will eventually face an incident. The ones worth trusting are the ones that **own it, fix it, and make users whole** — which is exactly what happened here. Contrast that with the platforms in our [scam guides](/scam-guides), where an "incident" is usually the moment withdrawals stop forever.

## Fees: use the Exchange, not the App

This is the single biggest mistake new users make on Crypto.com, so we'll be blunt about it.

The **App** (the simple, colourful buy/sell screen) bakes a **spread of roughly 0.5–1%+** into the price of every instant buy. It's convenient, and it's expensive. The **Exchange** (the pro platform on the same account) uses real order books with **maker/taker fees around 0.00%–0.075%**, scaling with your 30-day volume and how much CRO you stake.

For a one-tap £20 buy, the spread barely matters. For anything you'd call trading, it's the difference between keeping your gains and donating them. **Learn the Exchange.** If you're comparing venues, our [how to pick a safe crypto exchange](/blog/how-to-pick-safe-crypto-exchange-2026) guide covers what else to weigh beyond fees.

## Withdrawals and deposits — what we saw

Withdrawals are where platforms reveal themselves, so we test them rather than trust them.

In our checks, **crypto withdrawals cleared within minutes to a couple of hours** once the destination address was whitelisted. The first withdrawal to any **new** address is held for **24 hours** — that's the post-2022 security feature, not a stall, and you should welcome it. **Fiat withdrawals** landed in **1–3 business days** depending on the rails (SEPA, Faster Payments, ACH).

Deposits support bank transfer, card and crypto; card is instant, bank transfers are usually same-to-next-day. Crucially, we found **no pattern of withheld balances, surprise "fees to unlock," or vanishing support** — the behaviours that define the platforms we warn against. Always run a **small test withdrawal** first anyway; it's the cheapest insurance in crypto, and we explain why in [why withdrawal testing matters](/blog/why-withdrawal-testing-matters).

## The catch: it's custodial

Here's the honest limitation. When your coins sit on the Crypto.com App or Exchange, **Crypto.com holds the keys, not you**. That's true of every centralised exchange, and it's fine for funds you're actively trading or spending. It is **not** where serious, long-term holdings belong.

The good news is Crypto.com gives you the exit: the separate **DeFi Wallet** is a genuine self-custody app where you control your own keys. For anything you can't afford to lose, move it there or to a [hardware wallet](/crypto-wallets). Not your keys, not your coins — that rule doesn't bend for a big brand.

## Our verdict on Crypto.com

Strip away the adverts and Crypto.com is a **legitimate, licensed, well-resourced platform** that does the unglamorous things right: it's regulated, it publishes reserves, it pays withdrawals, and it made users whole when it was hacked. That earns it a **Trust Score of 82/100** and a green rating.

The deductions are real and worth repeating: it's **custodial**, the **App instant-buy is overpriced**, and the **card cashback has been cut hard** with the best tiers gated behind locked CRO. Use the Exchange, keep long-term holdings in self-custody, model the card on today's terms, and Crypto.com is a strong mainstream choice. Treat the marketing as information rather than advice, and you'll do fine.
