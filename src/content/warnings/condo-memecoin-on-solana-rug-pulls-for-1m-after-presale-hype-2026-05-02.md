## Quick summary

On 1 May 2026, the Solana based memecoin project Condo ($CONDO) collapsed. The anonymous developers executed a classic rug pull, stealing over $1 million in SOL from investors. This occurred just hours after a frenzied presale campaign.

The project website and social media accounts vanished instantly. This left thousands of holders with worthless tokens. This event serves as a stark reminder of the risks within the unregulated memecoin market.

## What happened

The Condo ($CONDO) scam followed a predictable but devastating pattern. The developers spent weeks building social media hype. They used aggressive marketing to attract retail investors. They promised massive returns to those who participated in a direct presale.

Investors were told to send SOL to a specific wallet address. In exchange, they were promised an airdrop of $CONDO tokens at launch. On 1 May, the token was listed on the Raydium decentralised exchange.

For a brief period, the price surged as more buyers entered the market. However, the developers held a massive portion of the supply. They simultaneously dumped their tokens and removed the liquidity they had provided.

The price of $CONDO crashed by more than 98 percent in minutes. The developers made off with approximately 5,500 SOL. This is not an isolated incident. A similar project with the same name rugged on the Base network just one week prior.

## Why this matters

This incident highlights the extreme vulnerability of the Solana ecosystem to exit scams. The low cost of launching tokens makes it a playground for bad actors. When a rug pull occurs, there is no central authority to reverse transactions.

The loss of $1 million impacts the entire community. It erodes trust in legitimate decentralised finance projects. Furthermore, these events often lead to secondary scams. Victims are frequently targeted by fake recovery services promising to get their money back.

## How the scam or exploit works

The Condo ($CONDO) scam relied on a four stage process. First, the team created artificial demand. They used bots and paid influencers to create a sense of urgency. This is often called FOMO, or fear of missing out.

Second, they used a direct to wallet presale. This is a massive red flag. Legitimate projects typically use smart contracts or reputable launchpads. Sending funds directly to a developer wallet provides zero protection for the buyer.

Third, they provided initial liquidity on a decentralised exchange. This allowed the token to be traded publicly. It gave the project an appearance of legitimacy. New investors saw the rising price and bought in, adding more SOL to the pool.

Finally, the developers executed the exit. They sold their tokens for the SOL in the pool and then withdrew the remaining liquidity. This left the pool empty of value. For a deeper look at these tactics, read our [crypto scam guides](/warnings).

## Red flags to check first

The Condo project was littered with warning signs. The most obvious was the anonymous nature of the team. No real names or professional backgrounds were provided. This anonymity allows developers to vanish without legal consequence.

The promise of guaranteed high returns is another major red flag. No legitimate investment can guarantee a 100x return. Any project using such language is likely a scam. High rewards always come with high risks.

The lack of a technical audit was also telling. Reputable projects have their code reviewed by third party security firms. Condo had no such oversight. You can check for these vulnerabilities using a [free crypto safety check](/reviews) before investing.

Finally, the recycled name should have caused concern. Scammers often reuse successful branding from previous thefts. Always check the [latest crypto scam warnings](/warnings) to see if a project name has been flagged before.

## What victims should do now

If you have lost funds in the Condo rug pull, you must act quickly. First, accept that the $CONDO tokens in your wallet are now worthless. Do not attempt to buy more in hopes of a price recovery.

Be extremely wary of anyone claiming they can recover your funds. These are almost certainly [crypto recovery scams](/blog/crypto-recovery-scams-warning). They will ask for an upfront fee or your private keys. No legitimate firm can reverse a blockchain transaction.

Document everything related to your investment. Save screenshots of transactions and social media posts. You should [report a crypto scam](/submit) to help warn others. This data is also useful for law enforcement investigations.

Report the incident to the [FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center](@@P0@@) or [Action Fraud UK](@@P1@@). While recovery is rare, reporting helps authorities track the movement of stolen funds. This can eventually lead to the freezing of assets on centralised exchanges.
