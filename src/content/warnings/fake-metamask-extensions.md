## Quick summary
Fraudulent browser extensions mimicking the MetaMask wallet have appeared on the Chrome Web Store. These malicious tools are designed to harvest seed phrases from users. Once a victim enters their recovery words, their funds are typically stolen within minutes.

This MetaMask (Impersonators) scam relies on visual deception. The fake extensions look nearly identical to the legitimate software. Users must exercise extreme caution when installing browser based wallets. Verifying the source through the official website is the only reliable way to stay safe.

## What happened

Security researchers and users have identified multiple fake MetaMask extensions listed on official browser stores. These listings often use the same logos and branding as the real product. They frequently appear in search results when users look for wallet software.

The primary goal of these extensions is to trick users into a phishing scam. When a user installs the fake software, they are prompted to import an existing wallet. This requires entering a secret recovery phrase. The fake extension then transmits this phrase to the attackers.

Once the attackers have the seed phrase, they gain full control over the associated blockchain accounts. This allows them to execute a wallet drainer script. These scripts automatically transfer all valuable assets to the criminal's address.

The speed of these thefts is often devastating. Victims report that their balances are emptied almost as soon as they finish the setup process. This suggests that the attackers use automated bots to monitor and drain compromised wallets in real time.

## Why this matters

This situation represents a critical crypto scam warning for all digital asset holders. MetaMask is one of the most popular tools for interacting with decentralised finance. Because it is so widely used, it is a primary target for sophisticated impersonation attacks.

The threat is particularly dangerous because it occurs at the point of entry. Users who believe they are following best practices by using self custody may still fall victim. If the software itself is compromised, the security of the underlying blockchain cannot protect the user.

Losses from these scams are usually permanent. Blockchain transactions are irreversible by design. There is no central authority to call to stop a transfer or reverse a fraudulent transaction once the seed phrase is compromised.

Furthermore, these fake extensions undermine trust in the entire ecosystem. Even experienced users can be fooled by a professional looking interface. Maintaining vigilance is essential to prevent the total loss of personal savings stored in digital wallets.

## How this scam or exploit works

The scam begins with search engine manipulation or social media promotion. Attackers often pay for advertisements that appear at the top of search results. These ads lead directly to the fake Chrome Web Store listing rather than the official site.

Once the user clicks the link, they see a page that looks legitimate. The attackers may even use fake reviews and high download counts to build a false sense of security. The user installs the extension thinking it is the genuine tool.

After installation, the fake extension mimics the standard onboarding flow. It asks the user if they want to create a new wallet or import an existing one. Most victims are trying to access their current funds, so they choose the import option.

The extension then provides a field for the twelve or twenty four word seed phrase. In a legitimate scenario, this phrase stays local to your device. In this scam, the software sends the words to a remote server controlled by the thieves.

With the seed phrase in hand, the attackers do not need the victim's computer anymore. They can recreate the wallet on their own devices. They then use automated tools to sweep all tokens, including small balances and staked assets.

## Red flags to check before connecting a wallet or depositing

The first red flag is the source of the download. If you found the extension through a search engine ad or a direct link in an email, it is likely a trap. Always navigate to the official website manually.

Check the developer information on the Chrome Web Store carefully. The legitimate extension is provided by metamask.io. Scammers often use slightly altered names or different domains that look similar at a first glance.

Look at the number of reviews and the date of the first review. A legitimate tool like MetaMask will have tens of thousands of reviews spanning several years. A fake version will often have a much smaller number or very recent entries.

Be wary of any extension that asks for your seed phrase immediately after an update. While legitimate imports do require the phrase, a sudden request for these words during normal use is a major warning sign of a phishing scam.

Another red flag is the quality of the text and images. While some fakes are high quality, others contain spelling errors or low resolution icons. If any part of the interface looks unofficial or broken, stop immediately and delete the extension.
