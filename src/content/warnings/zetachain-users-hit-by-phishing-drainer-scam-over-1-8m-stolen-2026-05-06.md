## Quick summary

A sophisticated phishing campaign has targeted the ZetaChain community, resulting in the theft of over 1.8 million dollars. Attackers used fraudulent Google advertisements to lure users to malicious websites. These sites deployed wallet drainer technology to siphon assets from over 160 victims.

The scam relies on the promise of a fake ZETA token airdrop. Users who connected their wallets and signed malicious permissions lost their funds instantly. This incident highlights the growing danger of sponsored search results in the digital asset space.

## What happened

In mid 2024, security researchers identified a surge in malicious activity targeting ZetaChain users. The attackers purchased sponsored slots on Google search results. When users searched for terms like ZetaChain airdrop, the top result often led to a fraudulent website.

These websites were designed to look identical to official ZetaChain portals. They featured professional branding and convincing user interfaces. The goal was to persuade visitors to connect their cryptocurrency wallets to claim a supposed reward.

Once a wallet was connected, the site prompted the user to sign a transaction. This signature was not for an airdrop. Instead, it granted the attackers permission to move assets. Within seconds, the victim's wallet was emptied of valuable tokens.

Data from on chain analysis shows that one individual lost approximately 420,000 dollars in a single transaction. The total losses across the community exceeded 1.85 million dollars. The official ZetaChain team confirmed that no such airdrop was active at the time.

## Why this matters

This event demonstrates that even experienced users can fall victim to well funded phishing campaigns. By using paid advertisements, scammers bypass the organic trust signals that users typically rely on. It proves that search engine rankings do not equal legitimacy.

The scale of the theft shows how lucrative these operations have become for cybercriminals. With nearly 2 million dollars stolen in a short period, attackers have more resources to fund future campaigns. This creates a cycle of increasingly sophisticated threats for the entire industry.

## How the scam or exploit works

The ZetaChain scam uses a technical tool known as a wallet drainer. This is a script embedded in a website that identifies the most valuable assets in a connected wallet. It then generates a specific request for the user to sign.

Many of these drainers now exploit the Permit2 standard. This standard was created to simplify token approvals, but scammers use it to gain broad permissions. A single signature can allow the attacker to withdraw multiple different types of tokens simultaneously.

The process begins with the bait, which is the fake airdrop. The user is under pressure to act quickly to avoid missing out. This psychological pressure often leads to a lapse in judgement when reviewing the technical details of the signature request.

Once the user signs, the drainer executes the transfer immediately. The assets are moved to a series of intermediary wallets before being sent to mixers or exchanges. This makes the funds extremely difficult to track or recover through traditional means.

## Red flags to check first

The most prominent red flag in this case was the use of sponsored search results. Legitimate crypto projects rarely need to pay for top placement on their own brand names. Always look for the organic search results located below the advertisements.

Another major warning sign was the timing of the airdrop. The official ZetaChain distribution had already concluded months prior. Any website offering a new claim for an old event should be viewed with extreme suspicion.

Users should also examine the URL of the website carefully. Scammers often use lookalike domains that replace one letter or use a different extension. For example, they might use a dot net or dot io address instead of the official dot com.

Finally, pay close attention to your wallet pop up. If a site asks for SetApprovalForAll or a Permit2 signature when you only expected to login, it is likely a scam. You can learn more about identifying these threats in our [crypto scam guides](/scam-guides).

## What victims should do now

If you have interacted with a suspicious ZetaChain link, you must act immediately. Your first priority is to revoke all active permissions. Use a tool like [Revoke cash](@@P0@@) to see which contracts have access to your funds and cancel them.

If your wallet has already been drained, do not send more funds to that address. The attackers may still have permissions that allow them to steal any new deposits. You should move any remaining assets to a brand new wallet with a fresh seed phrase.

Report the incident to the authorities. In the UK, you can contact [Action Fraud](@@P1@@). In the US, file a report with the [FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center](@@P2@@). This data helps law enforcement track the movement of stolen funds.

Be wary of anyone claiming they can recover your lost crypto for a fee. These are almost always recovery scams. You can read our [crypto recovery scam warning](/blog/crypto-recovery-scams-warning) to understand how these secondary predators operate.

## How to avoid similar crypto scams

The best way to stay safe is to avoid clicking on any sponsored links for crypto services. Bookmark the official websites of the platforms you use frequently. This ensures you always land on the correct domain regardless of search engine manipulation.

Consider using a dedicated browser for your crypto activities. Install security extensions that flag known malicious domains. These tools can provide an extra layer of defence by blocking access to phishing sites before you even see them.
