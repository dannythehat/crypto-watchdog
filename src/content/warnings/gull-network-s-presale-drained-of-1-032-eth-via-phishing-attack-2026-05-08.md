## Quick summary

On 24 May 2024, the Gull Network presale contract suffered a catastrophic loss. An attacker drained 1,032 ETH, worth approximately £3 million. This was not a complex hack of the code itself. Instead, it was a simple phishing attack on a single person.

The project owner lost control of their wallet. Because this one wallet held total power over the funds, the attacker emptied the contract instantly. This event highlights the extreme danger of centralised control in decentralised finance. Investors must understand that even "audited" projects can fail due to human error.

## What happened

Gull Network was a project launching on the Manta Network. It attracted significant attention and successfully raised over 1,000 ETH from retail investors. The funds were meant to build the ecosystem. However, blockchain security firms like Cyvers noticed suspicious activity on the presale address.

The "owner" wallet of the presale contract initiated a massive transfer. It moved 1,032.17 ETH to a fresh address. This address was `0x41700e12a6a163143701657c393343a3c10cf33c`. The Gull Network team later confirmed they were victims of a sophisticated phishing scam.

The attacker did not wait. They quickly moved the stolen assets through privacy mixers like Tornado Cash. This process obfuscates the trail of money. Once funds enter a mixer, they become nearly impossible to track or freeze. For the investors, the capital was gone in minutes.

## Why this matters

This incident is a textbook example of a "single point of failure". In the crypto world, we often talk about decentralisation. Yet, many new projects operate with a single person holding the keys to the vault. This creates a massive security hole for everyone involved.

If one person can be tricked, the entire community loses. This case shows that technical audits of smart contracts are not enough. You must also look at how the team manages the keys. Operational security is just as important as the code itself.

Furthermore, it damages the reputation of the broader ecosystem. When millions are lost to simple phishing, it scares away legitimate participants. It reinforces the idea that the space is a "wild west" where no one is truly safe. You can find more [latest crypto scam warnings](/warnings) to see how common this is.

## How the scam or exploit works

Phishing is a form of social engineering. The attacker likely contacted the project owner through a platform like Discord or Telegram. They might have posed as a partner, a developer, or a service provider. They then sent a malicious link.

This link would lead to a fake website. The owner was likely asked to sign a transaction or enter their seed phrase. Once they did this, the attacker gained full access to the wallet. This is known as a wallet drainer attack.

Because the presale contract was controlled by this single wallet, the attacker had "admin" rights. They simply called the function to withdraw the funds. There were no safety checks or delays. This is why we advocate for [crypto safety education](/blog) before investing in any presale.

## Red flags to check first

Investors often ignore warning signs when they are blinded by potential profits. The first red flag was the lack of a multi signature wallet. A professional project should require at least three out of five team members to approve any large movement of funds.

Another red flag was the lack of a time lock. A time lock ensures that any withdrawal takes 48 or 72 hours to process. This gives the community time to react if a wallet is compromised. Without a time lock, the money can vanish in a single block.

Finally, check the project's operational transparency. If they do not explicitly state how they secure the presale funds, assume they are using a single wallet. You can use our [free crypto safety check](/reviews) to help identify these structural risks in new projects.

## What victims should do now

If you lost money in the Gull Network scam, you must act quickly but cautiously. First, accept that the funds are likely gone. Do not let your emotions make you a target for a second time. Many people lose even more money trying to "recover" what was lost.

You must [report a crypto scam](/submit) to the relevant authorities immediately. In the United Kingdom, you should contact [Action Fraud UK](@@P0@@). In the United States, use the [FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center](@@P1@@). These reports help law enforcement track the attackers.

Be extremely wary of anyone claiming they can get your money back. These are almost always recovery scams. They will ask for an upfront fee to "hack" the attacker or "track" the funds. They will take your fee and disappear. Read our [crypto recovery scam warning](/blog/crypto-recovery-scams-warning) for more details.

## How to avoid similar crypto scams

The best way to stay safe is to avoid projects that use single owner wallets for large sums. Always ask the team for their multisig address. If they cannot provide one, do not send them your money. This is a basic standard for any legitimate operation.

Use a hardware wallet for your own holdings. While it would not have stopped this specific contract drain, it protects your personal assets from phishing.
